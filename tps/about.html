The Papers Say

Architecture & How It Works

What Is This?

The Papers Say is a fully automated daily podcast that reads and discusses Japan's top news stories in English, for an international audience. Every episode is generated end-to-end by AI — no human writes, records, or edits anything. It runs automatically at 5:50 AM Japan Standard Time every day, just before the Tokyo market opens.

The host rotates on a 3-day cycle — Sarah (American female), Christie (male), or Maya (British female). Each episode presents the day's most important stories from Japan's morning press in a warm, public-radio style, covering each story in plain English, explaining the context, and ending with one story from deeper in the paper.

The Papers Say is a companion show to Tokyo Open, which covers the market implications of the same morning's news.

Pipeline

Each episode is produced in four stages, taking roughly 10–12 minutes from start to finish.

1
Article Collection
A headless browser authenticates with a major Japanese news publication and scrapes the full morning newspaper edition. Typically 120+ articles are collected in Japanese, spanning the front page, general news, global markets, international, business, investment, and commodities sections. The browser session is kept alive between runs using a saved authentication state, so no login is required each morning. Takes ~4 minutes.
2
Story Selection (Claude Haiku)
All collected articles — in Japanese — are passed to Claude Haiku, which selects the 10–12 most newsworthy stories for an international English-speaking audience. Front-page articles are always included. For each selected story, Haiku writes a clear English headline and a 2–4 sentence summary covering what happened, the key figures and numbers, and why it matters. Takes ~10 seconds.
3
Script Generation (Claude Opus)
The selected story summaries are passed to Claude Opus, which writes a 1,200–1,500 word spoken monologue as Sarah. Stories are grouped thematically where they connect, with more time given to the most important ones. The script cites the source publication by name where it adds authority. The final segment is always a "story that caught my eye" — something from deeper in the paper that deserves more attention. Takes ~30 seconds.
4
Voice Synthesis & Publish
The script is split into paragraphs and each is synthesised using Google Cloud Text-to-Speech or ElevenLabs, depending on the host for the day. Sarah and Maya use Google Cloud TTS (en-US-Journey-F and en-GB-Journey-F respectively). Christie uses her ElevenLabs voice. Paragraphs are stitched together with short pauses using ffmpeg and the final MP3 is pushed directly to this GitHub Pages site. Takes ~8 minutes.

What Gets Included

  • All front-page stories
  • Major economic & political events
  • Corporate news & earnings
  • International affairs affecting Japan
  • Trade & policy developments
  • One "buried story" per episode

The newspaper is accessed via an authenticated headless browser. Articles are collected in their original Japanese and translated into English inline during the story selection step. The AI reads the full text of each article, not just the headline.

What It Is Not

The Papers Say does not track stock prices, give market forecasts, or make investment recommendations. It is a news summary show, not a financial show. For market implications of the same morning's news, listen to Tokyo Open.

Automation

The entire pipeline runs on GitHub Actions — no dedicated server required. A scheduled trigger fires at 5:50 AM Japan Standard Time every day. The generated MP3 is pushed directly to this GitHub Pages site via the GitHub API, overwriting latest.mp3. The episode date metadata is updated in episode.json, which the player reads to display the correct date.

Articles are sourced from a licensed Japanese news publication. This project has no affiliation with any of the companies, publications, or individuals mentioned in episodes.
