Good morning. I'm Sarah, and this is The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Saturday, June 20th, 2026.

We begin this morning with a story that would have been almost unthinkable a decade ago. Tokyo Electric Power — TEPCO, the utility at the center of the Fukushima nuclear disaster — is now in serious talks to bring in outside investors to rebuild itself. And not just any investors.

According to the Nikkei this morning, TEPCO is in advanced negotiations with five major bidders. There's SoftBank. There's the Japanese private equity firm Japan Industrial Partners. And then there are three of the biggest names in global finance — the American funds KKR and Blackstone, and Global Infrastructure Partners. The bids on the table exceed one trillion yen — that's about seven billion dollars — and some of those proposals would take TEPCO private altogether.

Here's why this matters. TEPCO is still carrying an enormous burden. It needs to find roughly five hundred billion yen every single year — for decommissioning the wrecked Fukushima reactors, and for upgrading Japan's aging power grid. That's a staggering, open-ended commitment. And so the company is doing something Japanese utilities almost never do — reaching outside, and to foreign capital, for help.

Now, this is sensitive. TEPCO is part of Japan's critical infrastructure. So the government plans to keep what are called "golden shares" — special shares that let it retain a veto over major decisions, no matter who else owns the company. It's a way of opening the door to private money while keeping a hand on the controls. The Nikkei calls this the largest restructuring of a Japanese utility through external capital in decades. Watch this one closely.

That theme — Japan opening up, and the state taking a more active role in the economy — runs right through this morning's paper.

Because on the front page, the Abe administration has laid out an extraordinarily ambitious industrial plan. The headline figure: ten and a half trillion yen over the next two decades for what's being called Physical AI. That means autonomous robots — machines that use artificial intelligence to move and work in the real world, not just on a screen. The government thinks the market for AI robots alone could reach sixty trillion yen by 2040.

And that's just one piece. The broader target is three hundred and seventy trillion yen in combined public and private investment across seventeen strategic sectors by 2040 — forty trillion for semiconductors, eighteen and a half trillion for aerospace, three trillion for ocean development. This is state-led industrial policy on a scale Japan hasn't attempted in a very long time. The bet is clear: Japan wants to manufacture its way back into global competitiveness, and it's willing to spend to do it.

Also from the front page — Japanese corporate boardrooms are quietly changing. After June's shareholder meetings, forty percent of major Japanese firms will have independent outside directors making up more than half of their boards. That's up eight points from last year. For decades, Japanese boards were insular, full of company insiders. This shift toward Western-style governance means more outside scrutiny of management, and stronger protections for investors. A slow change, but a real one.

Two more from the front page worth your attention. The cabinet has unveiled a draft amendment to the Imperial House Law. It would allow the adoption of male-line males from former imperial branches — but only those aged fifteen or older, so they can genuinely consent — to join the imperial household. They wouldn't have succession rights. A separate proposal would let female imperial members stay in the household after they marry. This is Japan inching, carefully, toward addressing a shrinking imperial family.

And starting in 2027, the new Ministry of Children and Family Affairs will require social media platforms to verify users' ages, rather than just trusting people to report their own. The goal is to shield younger users from harmful content — closing a gap on platforms like X.

Let's turn abroad, because there's real news here too.

The Nikkei reports that Iran has come out ahead in a ceasefire deal with the United States. Tehran gets to dilute its nuclear fuel inside its own borders, and — significantly — wins back the right to export oil. In return, Iran agreed only to sixty days of toll-free passage through the Strait of Hormuz, that vital oil chokepoint, with the longer-term arrangement to be negotiated later through Oman. The Nikkei frames this as a string of concessions from the Trump administration — driven, it suggests, by U.S. fiscal pressures heading into the midterm elections.

In Britain, Andy Burnham — the mayor of Greater Manchester — has won the Mayfield by-election in a landslide, beating Reform UK. That victory positions him as the strongest potential challenger to Prime Minister Keir Starmer, and Burnham has signaled he'd run for the Labour leadership if the pressure on Starmer comes to a head.

And in Switzerland — a country long defined by its anti-nuclear stance — parliament has voted to legalize building new nuclear plants, reversing a 2017 ban. Energy security after the invasion of Ukraine simply outweighed the old objections. Voters could still overturn it in a referendum, but the direction is striking.

One more international story with a direct Japan angle: Germany's Rheinmetall, Europe's largest defense manufacturer, is looking to set up weapons production in Japan through a joint venture. It would be the company's first defense plant outside Europe — a sign of how much closer Tokyo and Berlin are moving on security.

Now to business, and a remarkable windfall hiding in plain sight. The Nikkei reports that Nippon Life — Japan's big life insurer — holds a stake in SpaceX worth somewhere between one and five trillion yen. They got in more than ten years ago through early venture funds, and when the lockup expires after a SpaceX IPO in 2028, those gains could land. The paper calls it potentially Japan's largest tech venture-capital success ever.

And SpaceX is everywhere in the markets this morning. Japanese retail investors have been pouring money into it through a British fund, and that enthusiasm — plus relief over the Iran truce — helped push the Nikkei 225 to a record 71,250, a five-day winning streak. But there's a worry underneath. The Nikkei notes that Japanese capital is increasingly flowing into U.S. markets, and U.S. exchanges are now piloting 24-hour trading to capture Asian hours. The concern: that global money keeps concentrating in America, leaving Japan's own promising companies — what the paper nicely calls "raw gems" — overlooked.

Meanwhile, the yen slipped to 161.80 against the dollar, edging toward a 39-year low. A strong dollar, and no sign the Bank of Japan will tighten, leave the yen exposed. Markets are watching the 162 line — that's where intervention might come.

Two quick corporate notes before we close. Honda is facing supply-chain trouble: it cut its China output by sixteen percent while Toyota grew, and now suppliers like Jtekt are signaling they want to depend less on Honda. And Seven-Eleven is overhauling its franchise model — shorter contracts, lower fees — to make it easier to recruit new store owners.

And now, the one that caught my eye this morning. Buried deeper in the paper, a single town offering a glimmer of hope against Japan's relentless depopulation. The 2025 census found that twenty-six municipalities grew in population for the first time in decades. The standout is Minami-Horo Town, which grew by seven point eight percent — driven by aggressive child-care subsidies and family support. In a country shrinking almost everywhere, here's one small place that figured out how to bring families in, and keep them. It's worth remembering that depopulation isn't destiny. Policy can move the needle.
