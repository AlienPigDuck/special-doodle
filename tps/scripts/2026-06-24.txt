We begin with a quiet milestone that I think will turn out to be a big deal. A Portuguese drone company called TEKEVER — based in Lisbon — is setting up a manufacturing base here in Japan, in partnership with the trading house Marubeni. And here's why that matters: according to the Nikkei this morning, it's the first time a foreign defense contractor has built a manufacturing operation on Japanese soil.

Let me give you a sense of who TEKEVER is. They make long-range drones — aircraft that can fly more than two thousand kilometers, with technology designed to resist jamming. These are the kinds of drones that have been used in Ukraine, where, by one estimate, they've inflicted around three billion pounds — about 640 billion yen — in damage to Russian air defenses. So this is battle-tested hardware.

Now, why Japan, and why now? Two reasons. First, Japan has world-class sensor and robotics technology, and TEKEVER wants access to it. Second — and this is the policy piece — back in April, Tokyo revised its rules on transferring defense equipment abroad. For decades, Japan kept a very tight lid on exporting anything that looked like a weapon. That's been loosening, step by step. This deal is a direct result. TEKEVER plans to use Japan as a base to build a supply chain and export across Asia. So you're watching Japan quietly become a node in the global defense industry — something that would have been almost unthinkable a generation ago. Keep an eye on this one.

From the front lines to the front office — let's talk about the Bank of Japan, because there's a story here that's getting scholars genuinely worried.

The concern is about independence. Central banks are supposed to set interest rates based on the economy — inflation, growth, jobs — not based on what the sitting government wants. That separation is what gives a central bank credibility. But the Nikkei reports that under Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, Governor Ueda Kazuo has become noticeably more deferential. The paper says he's been consulting the government before raising rates — effectively seeking its blessing before making policy moves.

The comparison the article draws is with the US Federal Reserve under Jerome Powell. Powell stayed in regular dialogue with Congress, but he guarded the Fed's independence carefully. The worry here is that if the BOJ looks too politically aligned, it loses the market's trust. And if inflation fears start to build, that lost trust could force the bank into painful corrections at exactly the wrong moment.

This connects to another currency story today, and it's a striking one: the yen is under pressure that could push it to a 39-year low. Think about that — the weakest the yen has been against the dollar since the mid-1980s. The driver is the gap between Japanese and American interest rates. Even with the BOJ raising rates, the US Fed is holding its rates high, and money keeps flowing to the dollar. The paper notes that intervention — the government stepping in to buy yen — may not do much against forces this structural.

Now, two more from the front page, and both are significant in their own way.

The Fair Trade Commission — Japan's antitrust regulator — is changing how it reviews mergers. Going forward, it will explicitly weigh economic security as a factor. In plain terms: if a merger makes Japan's supply chains more stable or resilient, that benefit can now help offset competition concerns. The Nikkei points to industries like naphtha refining and shipbuilding, where strict antitrust rules had been blocking the kind of consolidation the government now sees as strategically necessary. The new guidelines take effect this summer. The balance to watch is whether "economic security" becomes a genuine standard — or a convenient excuse to wave through deals that hurt consumers.

And then a verdict that closes a long, difficult chapter. Japan's Supreme Court has upheld the dissolution of the Unification Church — formally, the Family Federation for World Peace and Unification. This is historic: the Nikkei notes it's the first time a religious organization in Japan has been dissolved over civil law violations, rather than through administrative action. The court found that the church's aggressive fundraising and the scale of its deception justified dissolution — and that doing so did not violate Japan's constitutional protection of religious freedom.

For listeners who haven't followed this: the church came under intense scrutiny after the 2022 assassination of former Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, when the gunman's grievances against the group, and its longstanding ties to Japanese politicians, came pouring into public view. This ruling is, in a real sense, the legal endpoint of that reckoning.

Let me move us through a few stories that touch everyday life.

There's a fascinating ripple effect from a proposed tax cut. If Japan lowers its food tax to one percent, the Nikkei reports that takeout and delivery businesses could take a real hit — a nearly 32 percent likelihood of negative impact. Why? Because if it gets cheaper to buy groceries and cook at home, fewer people order in. Restaurant chains like Skylark are responding by expanding what are called "ghost restaurants" — delivery-only kitchens with no dining room, no front of house. A small policy change, reshaping how dinner gets made.

On wages — the Labor Ministry is tightening the rules on minimum wage increases. Last year, different regions rolled out their raises as much as six months apart. Now, any region that wants to delay will have to justify it, with the goal of getting everyone to an October implementation. The aim is to narrow the gap between richer and poorer parts of the country.

And a number that says a lot about Japan's tourism boom: over 200 million low-duty import shipments last year — packages valued under 10,000 yen that come in nearly tax-free. That's up 20 percent, and the government now sees a loophole worth closing, with reforms coming in 2028. Related: the Finance Ministry wants all airport customs declarations done by QR code by 2030. No more paper forms — a sign of just how many visitors Japan is now handling.

Quickly overseas. There's a US-Iran ceasefire that's lowered the temperature in the Middle East — and you can see it in the markets. Dubai crude has dropped to a four-month low, around 70 dollars a barrel, down from a peak near 170 in March when traders feared the Strait of Hormuz might close. More than 400 tanker ships are now waiting for that passage to reopen. Though one note of caution: Israel disputes whether the ceasefire applies to its operations against Hezbollah in Lebanon.

And in Britain — political turmoil. Prime Minister Keir Starmer has resigned, which the Nikkei points out makes him the sixth British prime minister to leave office in a single decade. His likely successor, Andy Burnham, is expected to focus inward, on the economy — which could put a question mark over big international projects, including the fighter jet Britain is developing jointly with Japan and Italy.

And now, the one that caught my eye — and it's about money and minds.

The Nikkei takes a hard look at what Japan pays its top researchers, and the gap is stark. A professor at a national university earns about 10.5 million yen a year. A professor at Stanford? Around 25.6 million — more than double. Japan wants to produce 20,000 PhDs a year by 2030, but it's struggling to keep its best people from leaving. And here's the detail that stopped me: a government program meant to send young scientists abroad supports just 352 researchers, on stipends of around 8 million yen — which the paper notes falls below the minimum wage at some American universities. Keidanren, the big business federation, is urging 300 billion yen a year to fix this. It's a quiet crisis — the kind that doesn't make headlines until, one day, the discoveries are happening somewhere else.
