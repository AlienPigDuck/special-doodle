Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Monday, June 22nd, 2026, and there's a remarkable investigation leading the paper today, so let's get right into it.

The Nikkei has published the results of its own independent investigation into a Chinese chemical company that appears to have been running a sprawling criminal operation out of Japan. The company is called Hubei Amarvel Biotech, and it's tied to the illegal export of fentanyl — that's the synthetic opioid at the heart of America's overdose crisis. Some of its executives have already been convicted in the United States. But here's what the Nikkei uncovered: Amarvel was apparently using a Japanese subsidiary, a Nagoya-based company called FIRSKY, as a hub. And not just for drug trafficking logistics. For money laundering, and for a cryptocurrency fraud scheme worth hundreds of millions of yen.

Let me explain how this worked, because it's clever in a disturbing way. The operation registered fraudulent dot-J-P domain names — those are the web addresses ending in J-P, Japan's national domain — and used them to run fake cryptocurrency token operations. The Nikkei documented more than 120 transactions with entities already under U.S. sanctions, including the Wuhan Yuancheng Group, a major illegal chemical supplier whose leader carries a five-million-dollar American bounty.

Now, why Japan? Why would a Chinese trafficking network choose Tokyo and Nagoya as its base? The answer is uncomfortable. Just last month, in May, the U.S. Drug Enforcement Administration formally designated Japan a fentanyl transshipment point — a place these substances pass through on their way elsewhere. And the very things Japan is proud of — an open, sophisticated financial system, a trusted national web domain, close geographic ties to China — are exactly what make it attractive to criminals laundering money far beyond the drugs themselves. It's a sobering reminder that being a clean, well-run financial center has a shadow side. The reputable infrastructure is the appeal.

Let's turn from hidden money to very visible money — the luxury apartment market. The Nikkei reports this morning on the penthouses atop Tokyo and Osaka's so-called tower mansions — that's the Japanese term for high-rise luxury condominiums. They looked at 1,867 top-floor units in prime districts, and the headline figure is this: 56 percent of them were bought in cash. In Tokyo's Chiyoda ward, near the Imperial Palace, it's 69 percent. Cash, no mortgage, for apartments that in Shibuya and Minato now average 300 million yen — about two million dollars.

So who's buying? Wealthy entrepreneurs, foreign investors — about nine percent are foreign-owned, and among overseas-resident owners, Chinese residents hold nearly half — and a category the Nikkei wonderfully calls "tower mansion collectors." People who buy these units not to live in, but to flip, again and again, among the ultra-wealthy. It's a closed loop of money chasing money, and it's driving real estate inflation across Japan's biggest cities. The open question is sustainability. As the Bank of Japan raises interest rates, the investment math gets harder, and yet, for now, the game accelerates.

Now, here's a story I genuinely love, because it captures something about Japanese industry. TOTO — yes, the toilet company, maker of those famous heated, high-tech washlets — is investing 80 billion yen to become a supplier for the most advanced semiconductors on Earth. We're talking one-nanometer chips, the kind that power artificial intelligence. And how does a maker of bathroom fixtures get into cutting-edge chip materials? Ceramics. The firing technology TOTO perfected over decades making sanitary ware turns out to be exactly what's needed for next-generation semiconductor materials. And this isn't a side project anymore — the semiconductor materials division earned TOTO nearly 29 billion yen in operating profit this past fiscal year, more than half the company's total. A few years ago, that figure was close to zero. It's a beautiful example of a traditional manufacturer finding that its old craftsmanship is suddenly the future.

That theme — old Japanese giants reinventing themselves — runs through the business pages today. Panasonic, the electronics icon, is pursuing what the Nikkei describes as a corporate "dissolution," breaking up its bloated conglomerate structure into focused, independent business units after decades of underperformance. Investors approve — the stock hit record highs. And in retail, Japan's two largest consumer electronics chains, Yamada Holdings and Edion, have agreed to merge. The Nikkei calls it an "end-of-life" consolidation — a striking phrase. With the market saturated and shoppers gone online, the era of growth at any cost is simply over. Two giants combining not to expand, but to survive.

And now, the one that caught my eye — buried deep in the paper, and quietly fascinating. Japan's dentists are running out of cavities. The number of dental clinics has fallen from a 2016 peak of nearly 69,000 to about 66,000 today. And the reason is, frankly, a public health triumph: fluoride toothpaste and less sugar have cut childhood cavity rates by 80 percent since 1999. A whole profession built on drilling and filling is having to reinvent itself around prevention.

But here's the twist. Researchers at Kyoto University are developing an experimental drug that could actually grow new teeth — aimed first at people born missing teeth, but potentially transformative. If it works by 2030, it could upend dentistry entirely. The lesson the Nikkei draws is a quiet, profound one: when public health wins, entire professions can quietly collapse — and the question is whether anyone's ready for what comes next.
