We start this morning with something small enough to fit in your pocket, and worrying enough to prompt a nationwide survey. The humble USB memory stick.

Here's what happened. It's come to light that Japan's Self-Defense Forces — the country's military — were using virus-infected USB devices on their classified systems for nearly a year. The devices were manufactured in China. And this wasn't caught quickly. It went on for months, on some of the most sensitive networks the country has.

Now the response. The Ministry of Internal Affairs and Communications will survey all 1,788 of Japan's municipalities this month about how they use USB devices. And crucially, they're widening the net. Until now, Japan's security checks focused mostly on networking equipment — routers, the backbone of the internet. Now they're looking at USB devices too. That's a real shift in policy.

Why does this matter so much? Because in Japan, USB is still the primary way data moves around. People physically carry files from one machine to another on these little sticks. And Nikkei reports that municipal standards for managing them are dangerously inconsistent. Only ten percent of municipalities have strict criteria for the IT equipment they buy. Ninety percent don't. And here's the part that keeps security experts up at night — government and municipal networks are interconnected. So one compromised device, in one town hall somewhere, could cascade. A single infected stick becomes a national problem. It's a reminder that cybersecurity isn't just about firewalls and sophisticated code. Sometimes the vulnerability is a piece of plastic on someone's desk.

That theme of concentrated technological power runs through our next story too. A United Nations expert panel has released its first-ever report assessing the benefits and risks of artificial intelligence. And the headline is disparity. AI capability, the panel says, is concentrated in just two countries — the United States and China. And the wealth AI creates is flowing to a handful of nations and a handful of companies.

But the detail that stayed with me is about language. Major language groups are being left behind, and the consequences are life-threatening. The panel gives one chilling example: in Tigrinya — a language spoken by millions in the Horn of Africa — AI translation systems mistranslated the word "antibiotic" as "pesticide." Imagine that in a medical setting. A doctor, a patient, a prescription — and the machine tells you to take poison. The panel is calling for the creation of "AI Safety Research Institutes," and it warns that AI's growing power to persuade poses a genuine threat to democracy and human rights. A sobering first word from the UN on all this.

Well, here's a consequence almost nobody is talking about. Japan is running short of pharmaceutical containers. The little plastic tubs and vials that hold your creams and ointments and pills. They're made from naphtha, which comes from oil — and with oil supplies disrupted, the plastic has become scarce.

The numbers are striking. In Chiba Prefecture, eighty percent of surveyed pharmacies and hospitals report shortages. Twenty percent say it's actually disrupting their operations. And here's the part that would have been unthinkable in Japanese healthcare until recently: some medical facilities are now charging patients fifty-five to a hundred and ten yen — about fifty cents to a dollar — per container. Others are asking patients to bring back their used vials to be reused. Suppliers have ramped up production by twenty percent, and it still isn't enough. Lead times have stretched to eight, even twelve weeks.

Think about who this hurts most. Elderly patients, who rely on creams and ointments dispensed in exactly these containers. Policymakers are watching headline oil prices. But the real cost of that hundred-day war may be showing up quietly, in a pharmacy in Chiba, one empty tube at a time.
