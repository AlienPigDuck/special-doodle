Let's begin with a story that's quiet but, I think, deeply revealing about the country Japan is becoming. The Nikkei's lead this morning is about where people in Japan choose to die. As the population ages, more and more Japanese are dying outside hospitals — at home, or in care facilities — rather than in a hospital bed. That's a shift in preference, a desire to spend final days somewhere familiar. But here's what's striking. Where you live now seems to determine whether that wish is honored.

In Kanagawa, the prefecture just south of Tokyo, nearly twenty-seven percent of deaths now happen at home. In Kochi, on the island of Shikoku, that figure is under five percent. That's a twenty-point gap — and it's been widening for a decade.

Why? It comes down to whether home medical services exist where you live. In places with strong networks of visiting doctors and nurses, dying at home is a real option. In regions where hospitals still dominate, and where the older preference for institutional care runs deep, it simply isn't. The government has spent years promoting in-home care, but the Nikkei makes clear the job is half-finished. And this is the kind of inequality that doesn't show up in income statistics — it's an inequality of how your life ends. As Japan ages faster than any society in history, where you happen to live could shape your most personal moment. Something worth sitting with.

Now, from the end of life to the engines of the future — and a theme running right through today's paper, which is power. Electricity, specifically, and who's going to generate it.

Mitsubishi Heavy Industries is leading the front page. The company plans to invest more than a hundred billion yen, across Japan and the United States, to double its production of large gas turbines by fiscal 2030. And the driver here is artificial intelligence. AI data centers are enormously hungry for power, and companies are now building gas-fired plants right next to the data centers they feed. The Japanese utility JERA is doing exactly that. Mitsubishi Heavy's gas turbine business is booming — sales up twenty-five percent year-on-year, to nearly a trillion yen, with a record order backlog. It competes globally with America's GE Vernova and Germany's Siemens Energy, and right now, demand is its problem to solve, not find.

You see the same logic in an unexpected place — Honda. The Nikkei reports Honda has started making batteries at its Ohio plant not for electric cars, but for AI data centers. Honda had planned to build EVs there, but it canceled that after the Trump administration eliminated the tax credits that made American EVs viable. So those same factory assets — bought from LG Energy Solution for two and a half billion dollars — are being repurposed to store energy for data centers instead. By 2028, Honda expects to pivot again, this time toward hybrid vehicle batteries. It's a vivid illustration of how quickly companies are now rewriting their plans around two forces: AI demand, and Washington's shifting rules.

And speaking of AI, SoftBank's national push to build a homegrown Japanese AI now includes more than forty companies — Mitsubishi Electric, Sharp, Daiwa House, Kawasaki Heavy Industries among them, each chipping in around a hundred million yen. The goal is a large language model built for Japan by 2027, aimed not at chatbots but at what they call physical AI — robotics, machinery, the physical world. SoftBank, NEC, Honda, and Sony keep majority control. It's Japan's bid not to be left dependent on American models.

Turning to government and the economy. The National Personnel Authority will introduce a new kind of leave for national civil servants starting in 2027 — paid time off you can take without giving a reason, in flexible increments. The idea is to let workers handle childcare, disaster recovery, or simply life, without fear of being disciplined for the absence. It'll be part of August's formal recommendations. In a country famous for its workplace rigidity, this is a small but meaningful loosening.

There's also a quiet but important debate underway about Japan's bond market. As the Bank of Japan steps back from buying government bonds, someone has to step in to buy them. So the ruling Liberal Democrats and the opposition Democratic Party for the People are floating ideas to entice ordinary households — tax breaks, and letting individual bonds qualify for NISA, Japan's tax-free investment accounts. The thinking is that retail investors make steady, reliable buyers. It's a sign of how the era of easy central bank support is ending.

And on the world stage, Prime Minister Takaichi's government is launching eight digital infrastructure projects across India, Taiwan, Singapore, and Chile, using Japanese technology — including NTT's next-generation optical communications, known as IOWN. It fits squarely within Japan's Free and Open Indo-Pacific strategy: spreading Japanese tech as both commerce and diplomacy.

Internationally, the news is heavier. The fragile peace between the United States and Iran is wobbling. The two signed a ceasefire on June seventeenth, but on June twenty-sixth the U.S. struck Iranian military installations, and the next day Iran launched drone attacks on Bahrain in retaliation. It's the first major exchange since the ceasefire, and it threatens the final peace negotiations.

In Europe, Volkswagen — the continent's second-largest automaker — is considering cutting up to a hundred thousand jobs, fifteen percent of its global workforce. The Nikkei lays out why: labor costs of over three thousand euros per vehicle, sliding sales in America and China, and a company lagging behind Tesla and Chinese rivals on software and AI. A giant struggling to turn.

Two quick ones before I close. India is conducting its first paperless census since 2011 — three point one million workers visiting three hundred fifty million households, asking, for the first time in ninety-five years, about caste, and newly about smartphones and internet access. And China has warned Indonesia that new nickel mining rules could endanger up to five hundred billion dollars in Chinese investment — a reminder of how tightly China grips the world's critical minerals.

And now, the one that caught my eye — buried deeper in the paper, but I think it matters more than its placement suggests. Japan is overhauling its Arctic policy. Back in 2015, that policy was mostly about scientific research. Now Tokyo is rewriting it to prioritize resource security — rare earths, natural gas. It's nearly a hundred-and-eighty-degree turn. And the reasons tell you everything about this moment: the energy crisis around the Strait of Hormuz, China's so-called Ice Silk Road ambitions in the polar north, and a Russia increasingly isolated. Japan, which imports almost all its energy, is now looking to the top of the world for diversification and security. A frozen frontier, suddenly central to the great game of resources. Keep an eye on it.
