Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Wednesday, the twenty-second of July, 2026, and today we begin with a genuine turning point in how Japan thinks about money.

For twenty-five years, the guiding principle of Japanese economic policy has been caution. Balance the books. Don't spend what you can't afford. Japan carries the heaviest government debt in the developed world, and so the phrase you'd hear again and again was "fiscal consolidation" — the slow, patient effort to bring spending and revenue back into line.

Well, yesterday, Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's government threw that principle out.

Nikkei reports this morning that the cabinet approved a landmark basic economic policy that abandons the so-called primary balance target — the goal of not borrowing to cover day-to-day spending. In its place, the government will focus on something different: keeping the ratio of debt to the size of the economy stable, and getting there not by cutting, but by growing.

And the ambition here is enormous. There's a new framework — they're calling it "Strong and Prosperous Japan" — that would pour more than 370 trillion yen into 17 strategic sectors by the year 2040. That's roughly triple what Japan spent this fiscal year. The bet is that this investment lifts the economy — a target of three percent nominal growth, one percent real growth — and that growth, in turn, takes care of the debt.

It's a big bet. And the markets noticed immediately. Long-term interest rates in Japan briefly touched 2.9 percent — a thirty-year high. Now, why does that matter? Because when a country already owes as much as Japan does, and it announces it's going to borrow and spend far more, investors start asking a nervous question: can they really pay all this back, and will it stoke inflation? Higher interest rates are the market's way of asking that question out loud.

There are signs of that anxiety elsewhere in the paper. Foreign investors sold 108.5 billion yen of Japan's very long-term government bonds in June — their first selling in two months. Inflation expectations have climbed to just over two percent, the highest since 2015. And the odds of the Bank of Japan raising rates in October are now running around seventy percent. So Takaichi is opening the spending taps at the very moment the central bank may be tightening. That tension is going to define Japanese economics for the rest of the year.

And it shows up in the currency, too. The yen has weakened to the 163 level against the dollar — a thirty-nine-and-a-half-year low. Part of that is Japan's own story, but part is external: escalating military tension between the United States and Iran has sent investors rushing to the safety of the dollar, and it's raised worries about Japan's oil import bill. More on that in a moment.

Let me turn to two front-page stories that, in different ways, are about Japan choosing to stand apart from — or lean into — the rest of the world.

The first is quietly technical but genuinely important for anyone comparing Japanese companies to global ones. Japan's accounting standards body decided yesterday to keep depreciating something called "goodwill" — that's the premium a company pays when it buys another business, above the value of its actual assets. Most of the world, under the international and American standards, no longer writes that down gradually. Japan will continue to. The reasoning? Japanese firms carry very little goodwill on their books — a median of one percent of equity, compared with twenty or thirty percent in the US and Europe — so the sudden, brutal write-downs that hit foreign companies are less of a concern here.

But there's a real cost to going your own way. It makes Japanese firms harder for international investors to compare. And Nikkei gives us a vivid illustration of exactly what Japan is trying to avoid: Nikon just reported a record goodwill impairment charge of 612 billion yen under the international rules — a single enormous hit of the kind Japan's approach is designed to prevent.

The second front-page story is about the sky. Japan, Britain and Italy have formalized Canada's status as an observer in their joint fighter jet project — the Global Combat Air Program, aiming for a next-generation combat aircraft by 2035. A one-trillion-yen development contract has been signed. And here's what's telling: Japan wants to sell this technology to Germany, Australia and Singapore, to recoup its costs and share the risk. That's a deliberate break from the past. You may remember the Mitsubishi SpaceJet — Japan's attempt to build a passenger plane almost entirely on its own. It failed. This time, collaboration is the strategy, not self-reliance. And it comes as Japan loosens its rules on exporting defense equipment.

Speaking of reaching outward — Japan is deepening ties across Southeast Asia. There's a sixteen-billion-dollar energy framework called "Power Asia" aimed at energy security across the region. And Indonesia has become a real bright spot for Japanese manufacturers. Under President Prabowo's government, a massive village cooperative program is buying commercial trucks by the tens of thousands. Mitsubishi Fuso has secured an order for twenty thousand trucks and is doubling production, moving to two shifts. Hino has landed an order for ten thousand. For Japan's truckmakers, that's a very welcome piece of good news.

Now, one story from the paper that speaks to a quieter kind of crisis at home. We hear a lot about Japan's shortage of new housing — but Nikkei points to a hidden risk in the buildings that already exist. Japan has around 148 million buildings over forty years old. By 2045, that's projected to nearly triple. And when the management of an aging apartment complex breaks down, the building can simply decay. The paper describes a complex in Fukuoka abandoned for more than thirty-five years after a developer dispute froze all decision-making. But there's hope in the story too: a fifty-five-year-old building in Tokyo that residents revived themselves, through self-governance and long-term planning. The lesson is that age isn't destiny — community commitment is what determines whether a building lives or dies.

And that brings me to the one that caught my eye — buried deeper in the paper, and honestly one of the most quietly consequential stories of the week.

During the Middle East crisis, when the Strait of Hormuz was closed and the world lost something like 1.3 billion barrels of oil supply, you'd expect prices to spike out of control. They didn't. And Nikkei explains why: China.

Between February and June, China deliberately slashed its monthly crude oil imports — from 12.58 million barrels down to 7.15 million. It leaned on domestic coal, drew down its reserves, and restricted refined-product exports. In effect, Beijing offset roughly forty percent of the lost supply — not by producing more, but by choosing to consume less.

We've long thought of OPEC as the power that moves oil markets by adjusting production. This suggests China now has comparable power from the other direction — as a "demand-side adjuster." And that raises a real question for the years ahead: if Beijing can steady global energy markets by choosing to pull back, it can also unsettle them by choosing not to. Energy, it turns out, may be Beijing's next form of leverage.
