Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Tuesday, the fourteenth of July. I'm glad you're here.

Let's start with a story that gets to the heart of what Japan is, economically. For decades, when the world thought of Japanese industry, it thought of cars. Toyota, Honda, Nissan. And this morning, Nikkei is sounding an alarm about that industry — the word the paper uses is "existential."

Here's the situation. Japanese automakers are losing ground to Chinese manufacturers, and they're losing it fast. So fast, in fact, that Honda, Toyota, and Nissan are increasingly building their cars on Chinese-made platforms, using Chinese components. Think about what that means. These are companies that built their reputations on controlling their own technology, down to the last bolt. Now they're leaning on their rivals for the core of the machine.

Why is this happening? Speed, mostly. Nikkei reports that Chinese EV makers are developing new vehicles roughly twice as fast as their Japanese counterparts. They've got AI-driven manufacturing, tightly integrated supply chains, and they've captured market share by moving quickly and cutting costs. The numbers tell the story: China's share of the global auto market is projected to hit 25 percent by 2026 — nearly overtaking Japan's 26 percent. That's a gap that used to be a canyon, closing to almost nothing.

And the stakes here are enormous. Japan's auto industry employs 5.59 million people. It generates more than 10 percent of the country's entire GDP. So when Nikkei warns of "structural decline," it's not talking about one company's bad quarter. It's talking about the backbone of the Japanese economy.

The advice from industry experts in the piece is striking. Rather than trying to beat China head-on, they suggest Japanese firms should learn from China — study how they move so fast — and then redirect their energy toward markets where Japan still has an edge. Places like the United States and India, where the Japanese reputation for trust and reliability still counts for something. It's a humbling prescription. Japan spent decades as the teacher. Now it's being told to take notes.

Now, from an industry in trouble to one having its best moment in a generation. Because there's another story on the front page that's almost the mirror image.

Mitsubishi UFJ Financial Group — Japan's largest bank — has just become the most valuable company on the Japanese stock market. Its market capitalization hit 42 trillion yen on July 13th. And here's the remarkable part: no Japanese bank has topped these rankings since Sumitomo Bank did it back in 1986. Forty years. An entire generation of bankers came and went without a single one of them leading the market.

What changed? In a word, interest rates. For decades Japan lived with rates near zero — sometimes below it. That's brutal for banks, because banks make money on the spread between what they pay for deposits and what they earn on loans. Squeeze that spread to nothing and there's no profit to be had. Now, with rates rising, that spread is back, and the profits are flowing.

But Mitsubishi UFJ didn't just wait for rates to save it. More than half its operating earnings now come from overseas — the U.S. and Asia. Its stake in Morgan Stanley alone contributes somewhere between 20 and 30 percent of group profit. That's a bank that spent the lean years building elsewhere.

A note of caution, though, and it comes from the bank's own executives. Globally, Mitsubishi UFJ still ranks only eighth among the world's systemically important banks — well behind American giants like JPMorgan Chase. And as one executive put it, the real test comes when rate increases stall. Anyone can look good with the wind at their back.

Speaking of interest rates — they're causing trouble elsewhere in the paper, too. The government has actually delayed announcing its basic economic policy. In Japan this document has a wonderful nickname: the "honebuto no hoshin," which translates roughly as the "big-boned policy" — the fiscal backbone, the framework for the year. Well, the backbone is on hold, because long-term interest rates just breached 2.9 percent. That's a thirty-year high, and it's spooked markets worried about runaway government deficits — and even about whether the Bank of Japan can stay above the political fray.

Let me stay with the economy for one more, because it touches everyone's pocket. Nikkei reports that the price of DRAM memory chips has jumped sixfold in a single year. Sixfold. The culprit is the insatiable demand for AI servers, which are gobbling up the world's memory supply. And that's now hitting your phone. The average smartphone price is rising by 15,000 yen — to around 60,000 yen. Global smartphone shipments are expected to fall by 200 million units, the steepest drop on record. So the AI boom you keep hearing about? It's quietly making your next phone more expensive.

Overseas, the news is heavier. In the Middle East, the standoff over Iran has escalated sharply. Iran's Revolutionary Guards announced they'd re-closed the Strait of Hormuz on July 12th — that's the narrow waterway through which a huge share of the world's oil passes. The U.S. responded with airstrikes on Iranian air defenses and missile bases. Shipping traffic has dropped to a one-month low. And this is all despite a ceasefire that was signed just weeks ago, on June 17th. Right now, neither side seems willing to step back.

In Israel, Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has called a snap election for October 27th, framing it as a vote of confidence in himself. The polls aren't kind: his Likud party is projected to win just 21 seats — one behind the centrist Yesh Atid. That points to messy coalition negotiations ahead, and raises fears of further military escalation in Gaza and Lebanon.

And there's a story here that hits uncomfortably close to home for Japan. The New York Times, cited by Nikkei, reports that Russian military intelligence has been running a spy ring in Tokyo — GRU operatives posing as Aeroflot airline staff. Their job? Funneling Japanese semiconductors, industrial machinery, and communications gear back to Russia through third countries. The most sobering figure: Ukrainian officials estimate that 90 percent of Russian missiles now contain Japanese components. Ninety percent. It's a stark reminder that in modern warfare, the supply chain is the battlefield — and that export controls have gaps big enough to fly a plane through.

A couple of quick business notes before we finish. Oriental Land, which runs Tokyo Disney Resort, is raising its top-tier one-day ticket to 12,400 yen for peak days starting this October — up 1,500 yen, blamed on rising labor costs. And LINEYahoo has disclosed that 7.1 million user records leaked from two of its gaming apps over nearly four years, through a misconfigured data transmission. The data was encrypted, but it's another dent in the company's reputation for oversight.

And now, the one that caught my eye — and it's a lovely, quietly important story tucked into the front page.

Japan's railways are teaming up. Six of the major JR passenger companies — East, West, Central, Kyushu, Hokkaido, and Shikoku — along with private railways like Tobu, are agreeing to standardize more than a thousand vehicle components. Motors, power systems, pantograph parts — the pantograph being that arm on the roof of the train that reaches up to the overhead wire.

Why does this matter? Because domestic train production in Japan has fallen 8 percent over a decade. The suppliers who make these specialized parts are struggling with labor shortages and thin profits, and some are simply disappearing. By agreeing on shared specifications, the railways can pool their orders, keep those suppliers in business, cut costs, and — here's the clever part — make it easier to share parts during a disaster. Up to twenty operators nationwide may eventually join in.

I love this story because it's so Japanese in the best sense. Fierce competitors, quietly cooperating to protect something they all depend on. No drama. Just the trains running on time, for another generation.
