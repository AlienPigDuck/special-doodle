It's Tuesday, the thirtieth of June, 2026, and there's a lot moving this morning — the yen at a level we haven't seen in nearly forty years, companies bracing for a new kind of cyberattack, and a quiet resurgence of a children's spinning top in Hong Kong. Let's get into it.

We'll start with the lead story, because it's a glimpse of where things are heading. Nikkei this morning reports that Japanese companies are preparing for a new era of cyberattacks — attacks powered by artificial intelligence. In a survey of one hundred major company leaders, eighty-one percent said they plan to significantly increase what they spend on cybersecurity. And the reason is the arrival of advanced AI systems, like Anthropic's Claude Mythos, that could be turned to malicious ends.

Now, what exactly are they afraid of? Two things, mostly. The first is what's called a zero-day exploit — that's a software flaw that hackers find before the company that made the software even knows it exists, which means there's no patch, no fix ready. Nearly ninety-six percent of these executives flagged that as a top worry. The second is the supply chain. About two-thirds fear an attack coming not through their own front door, but through one of their suppliers.

And that fear isn't theoretical. Nikkei reminds us of what happened in 2022, when an automotive parts supplier called Kojima Press was hit by a cyberattack — and the knock-on effect forced Toyota to halt every one of its domestic factories. One small supplier, and the country's largest automaker goes dark. So companies are now deploying AI systems to defend against AI attacks — machine against machine — while quietly accepting a hard truth: you can't stop every attack. The new goal is to keep the business running even when one gets through.

Now to the front page, and to a number that will sting if you're holding yen this morning. On June 29th, the Japanese currency weakened to 161.97 against the U.S. dollar. That's the weakest the yen has been in thirty-nine and a half years. It just edged past the previous low from July of 2024.

Here's what's strange about that. The Bank of Japan actually raised its interest rate to one percent back on June 16th — and normally a rate hike strengthens a currency. But the yen keeps falling anyway. Why? Two forces. On the American side, the Federal Reserve may raise rates one or two more times this year, and the U.S. economy looks robust, so dollars are attractive. On the Japanese side, even after that rate hike, Japan's real interest rates remain very low — and Nikkei points to a shift in Prime Minister Takaichi's financial policy toward quantitative easing, which has encouraged yen-selling since last autumn. A weak yen is a double-edged thing: good for exporters, painful for anyone importing food, fuel, or anything else.

And speaking of the Prime Minister, she's heading to India this week, where she's expected to announce a joint clean-energy agreement. Japan and India will support green ammonia production — and let me explain what that means, because it's central to Japan's energy strategy. Ammonia can be burned as a fuel without releasing carbon dioxide, and if you make it using hydrogen from renewable energy, it's "green." The Japanese engineering firm IHI will partner with India's ACME Group to produce around 400,000 tons of it a year, at an investment of roughly 480 billion yen. The Japanese government will subsidize the gap between green ammonia and cheaper conventional fuel over fifteen years. It's one piece of a much bigger picture — and we'll come back to that picture in a moment.

Let me turn to Iran, because the economic toll there is staggering. Iran's government has announced a ceasefire with the United States, but the damage from the conflict is estimated at up to 300 billion dollars — about 48 trillion yen. To put that in perspective, that's roughly the size of Iran's entire annual economy. Gone. The rial has collapsed, and inflation in May hit seventy-seven percent year-on-year. Nikkei notes that even kebab — an everyday food — has doubled in price. The regime has issued a "victory declaration" through state media, but behind that, it's tightening its grip as public anger builds.

And the conflict isn't cleanly over. The June 26th ceasefire actually frayed within days, with both sides trading missile strikes before agreeing again to pause. Iran has used the Strait of Hormuz as leverage, blocking that vital shipping lane and pushing global crude oil prices higher. Negotiations resume in Doha today — but Nikkei is frank that deep distrust runs on both sides.

Now, I promised to return to the bigger picture, and here it is. Prime Minister Takaichi has unveiled an enormous growth strategy — a roadmap for 370 trillion yen in combined government and private investment across seventeen strategic sectors by 2040. That covers AI, semiconductors, and energy like the ammonia project I mentioned. This is a real philosophical shift: away from fiscal austerity, toward the state actively investing in chosen industries. Foreign investors are watching closely, and some are uneasy about whether Japan can afford it. Combine that fiscal expansion with the quantitative easing leanings I mentioned, and you start to understand why the yen is where it is this morning.

A couple of related threads. China has added twenty more Japanese firms to its sanctions list — defense and space companies — in retaliation for Takaichi's remarks about a potential Taiwan contingency. That brings the total to forty targeted companies. And at home, a government advisory council has declined to fully deregulate ride-sharing — the kind you'd see in much of the world. Instead, it sided with the taxi industry. Nikkei frames this bluntly as an example of business lobbying winning out over reform.

From across the Pacific, there's an unusual story about the Federal Reserve. The new chair has done something striking: eliminated forward guidance — those signals the Fed traditionally gives about where interest rates are headed. The argument is that too much transparency actually weakens policy. It's a sharp break from the era of Bernanke and Powell, and Nikkei draws the comparison to Alan Greenspan, the famously cryptic Fed chair of the past. For markets used to being told what's coming, this means more uncertainty, and likely more volatility.

A few quicker items from around the world. In South Korea, Samsung and SK Hynix will pour a combined 83 trillion yen into four new domestic chip factories by 2040 — doubling memory chip capacity within five years, all to feed the hunger of AI data centers. In Taiwan, nineteen major IT firms, including TSMC, posted a thirty-eight percent jump in May sales, driven by the same AI server boom. And in India, Reliance's Jio is preparing what could be a record IPO, aiming to raise around 610 billion yen to upgrade five hundred million customers to 5G.

There's also tragic news from Venezuela, where a magnitude-7 earthquake on June 24th has killed more than fourteen hundred people and displaced over fifty thousand — a brutal blow to a country already buried under inflation above five hundred percent.

And one note close to home: Japan's Health Ministry plans to require supplement makers to report health incidents to regulators. That follows the 2024 scandal involving Kobayashi Pharmaceutical's red koji products — red koji being a fermented rice ingredient — which was linked to serious illness. New oversight, born of hard lessons.

And now, the one that caught my eye. From Hong Kong, Nikkei brings us a story about Beyblade — the spinning-top battling toy made by Takara Tomy that many of us associate with children. Well, in Hong Kong, it's the twenty-six-year-olds who are buying them. Young adults who grew up on the game are now spending freely on collectibles and tournaments. The paper calls it "nostalgia consumption" — and there's something poignant in the explanation. Amid years of social upheaval and uncertainty, these young people are reaching back for something carefree from childhood, a simpler kind of joy they can control. A spinning top, of all things, as a small refuge. There's a lesson in that for anyone thinking about what people really buy, and why.
