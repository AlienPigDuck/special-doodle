We'll start with a story that touches almost everyone in Japan, whether they realize it or not. Nikkei reports this morning that PayPay — the mobile payment giant backed by SoftBank — and Seven & i Holdings, the company behind 7-Eleven and Ito-Yokado, are finalizing a deal to merge their customer databases. And when I say big, I mean big. PayPay brings around seventy-four million registered users. Seven & i's "7iD" — that's its own membership system — adds another thirty-nine million. Put them together, and you get Japan's largest points ecosystem, with more than a hundred million users.

Now, if you've never lived in Japan, the intensity of the loyalty-points world here can be hard to grasp. Points aren't just a nice bonus. They're a whole economy. People choose where to shop, which credit card to carry, even which phone carrier to use, based on which points they can earn and where they can spend them. So this merger isn't just housekeeping. It's a land grab.

And the money reflects that. SoftBank and PayPay are each weighing investments of around a hundred billion yen into Seven & i, with an announcement expected before the end of this month. The goal is to lock customers in — to use AI to analyze what you buy and nudge you toward buying more. And it's happening because everyone's doing it. FamilyMart has tied up with Rakuten. Lawson runs on the Ponta points system. So the convenience stores of Japan are quietly lining up behind rival points empires. Where you buy your morning coffee is becoming a statement of allegiance.

Let's stay with consolidation, because it's a real theme in the paper today. Down in Ehime Prefecture, on the island of Shikoku, two major regional banks — Iyo Bank Holdings and Ehime Bank — plan to merge by April of 2027. Together they'll hold assets of around twelve trillion yen, putting them in Japan's top twenty regional banking groups. Both names survive, under a single holding company. The strategic prize here is ship finance — lending to the shipbuilding industry, which is a regional specialty in that part of Japan.

And this fits a national pattern. Japan's regional banks have been under pressure for years — a shrinking, aging population means fewer customers and thinner margins. So they're merging to survive. Aichi Financial and Thirty-Three Financial wrapped up their talks back in May. Shizuoka and Nagoya Bank are planning a merger for 2028. The map of Japanese regional banking is being redrawn, prefecture by prefecture.

The same instinct is reshaping the audit world. Nikkei reports that Gyosei and Sanyu — two audit firms — have agreed to merge by July 2027, forming a new entity called BDO Japan. It'll top a hundred billion yen in revenue and audit around two hundred listed companies. Why does this matter? Because Japan has had a string of corporate accounting scandals, and that's put pressure on auditors to do better. Both firms say they're investing heavily in AI to catch fraud. It's the largest audit shakeup in three years.

Now to politics, and a phrase you're going to be hearing a lot: the "bone-thick shock." That's a nod to Japan's annual fiscal blueprint, nicknamed the "bone-thick policy." Nikkei surveyed economists, and seventy percent of them doubt that Prime Minister Takaichi's government can hit its central target — stabilizing Japan's debt-to-GDP ratio. The skeptics say the growth assumptions are simply too rosy. Japan carries the heaviest government debt burden in the developed world, so this is not a small doubt. And it connects to another finding: forty-four percent of economists don't believe the government's ambitious three-hundred-and-seventy-trillion-yen public-private investment plan — for AI, semiconductors, and green energy — can actually be deployed effectively. The worry is government picking the wrong winners, and crowding out private money.

You can hear those same anxieties in the bond market. Nikkei reports that yields on two-year Japanese government bonds have hit a thirty-one-year high — one and a half percent. Investors increasingly expect the Bank of Japan to raise rates as soon as October, with markets now pricing that at around eighty percent. After decades of near-zero rates, Japan is edging into a genuinely different monetary world.

Let's turn abroad, where energy is the headline. Pro-Iran Houthi militants in Yemen have declared renewed enforcement of a Red Sea blockade. That threatens the Bab al-Mandeb Strait, a chokepoint that carries about sixteen percent of the world's crude oil exports. Brent crude briefly pushed above a hundred dollars a barrel — the first time since May. Goldman Sachs warns that if disruptions drag on into 2027, we could see a hundred and twenty dollars or more.

And Europe is feeling energy pain from a different direction. A record heat wave has pushed temperatures above forty degrees Celsius — that's over a hundred and four Fahrenheit — in France and Germany. The heat is actually cutting power output, because nuclear and hydroelectric plants need water for cooling and generation. German baseload electricity prices jumped over nine percent; Dutch natural gas rose nearly five. A reminder that extreme weather isn't just a climate story — it's an economic one.

A few quicker international notes. The Trump administration has signed a civil nuclear cooperation deal with Saudi Arabia, offering Westinghouse reactors and — notably — enrichment pathways without the non-proliferation safeguards that were standard in past agreements. Nikkei flags concern about a possible "nuclear domino" in the Middle East. And closer to home, the U.S. Trade Representative has signaled new tariffs on Japan — twelve and a half percent, under a trade law called Section 301 — over what Washington calls inadequate forced-labor safeguards, effective today.

In corporate news, IBM cut its 2026 growth forecast, because customers are pouring money into AI servers and memory rather than the traditional IT services IBM sells — its core infrastructure division actually shrank seven percent. And South Korea posted strong growth, three-point-seven percent in the second quarter, riding a semiconductor boom led by Samsung and SK Hynix.

And that brings me to the one that caught my eye. Tucked into the business pages, Nikkei has a lovely piece on "hidden" shipbuilding stocks — the obscure suppliers riding Japan's shipyard revival. My favorite is a company called Moriya Transport Machinery. It's an elevator maker — the paper actually calls it "ancient" — and get this: only about one percent of its revenue comes from ship orders. One percent. And yet investors are buying it as a shipbuilding play. There's also a bearing specialist and a paint company that coats large vessels.

Why the excitement over such tiny exposures? Because shipbuilding runs on an enormous, slow cycle — Nikkei describes it as twenty-five years long — and the Japanese government has set a goal to double the country's shipbuilding capacity by 2035. So investors are hunting for any thread connected to that recovery, even a company that mostly makes elevators. There's something very human in that — the hope that a rising tide really does lift all boats, even the ones just building the elevators inside them.
