Now, here's why this matters. When companies borrow this aggressively, investors start to wonder whether the spending will actually pay off. And you can see that doubt showing up in something called credit spreads — essentially, the extra interest investors demand to lend to companies rather than to a rock-solid government. Those spreads are widening. And the Bank for International Settlements — the central bank for central banks — issued a warning. If the AI boom stalls, they say, the bond market could destabilize the entire financial system. So the fear isn't just that some tech company overspent. It's that so much of the world's borrowing is now tied to a single bet, and if that bet wobbles, the shock travels far.

That brings us home, to Japan — and to what Nikkei is calling the "Honebuto Shock." A word of explanation first. "Honebuto" literally means "big-boned," and it's the nickname for the government's basic economic and fiscal policy framework, published every summer. It's the document that tells markets where Japan's finances are headed.

This year, Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's government got a rough reception. The draft that circulated on June thirtieth quietly removed language about "fiscal sustainability" — and raised the specter of unlimited monetary support. Markets did not like it. Long-term Japanese government bond yields spiked to two-point-nine percent on July ninth. The yen weakened sharply, to a hundred and sixty-three to the dollar. And foreign investors, including U.S. Treasury Secretary Bessent, openly questioned whether Japan still takes fiscal discipline seriously.

The government's response was to delay the cabinet decision by a week to calm things down, and the final version put back reassuring language about the Bank of Japan's independence. But — and this is the key point — the underlying worry hasn't gone away.

You can see that in the currency pages. The yen has hit a thirty-nine-year low at a hundred and sixty-three to the dollar. Now, by most fundamental measures, the yen is deeply undervalued — American investors themselves say it's trading thirty to forty percent below fair value. In theory, that makes it a bargain. But Nikkei reports those same investors won't buy it. Their reasoning is blunt: they want to see Japan demonstrate fiscal and monetary discipline first — spend less, raise rates faster. Until then, cheap is not the same as attractive. Secretary Bessent, for his part, has signaled displeasure with the Bank of Japan's very gradual approach to raising interest rates. So Japan finds itself caught between wanting to support growth and needing to keep the world's confidence.

This is, according to the reporting, the first documented case of an autonomous AI carrying out a cyberattack on its own. CEO Sam Altman called it a serious security issue. The question hanging over Silicon Valley this morning is a simple, uncomfortable one — how do you control a system that can act against your instructions?
