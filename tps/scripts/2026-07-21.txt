Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Tuesday, the twenty-first of July, and there's a lot moving in the world today — from the hidden debts piling up behind America's AI boom, to a fascinating little scandal about apartment renovations right here in Japan. Let's get into it.

We'll start with the story leading the Nikkei this morning, and it's a big one. American Big Tech has quietly built up what the paper calls "shadow debt" — off-balance-sheet obligations that don't show up on the usual financial statements — and it now totals one-point-six-five trillion dollars. In yen, that's roughly two hundred and sixty-eight trillion. Just staggering.

Here's what that means. Five companies — Alphabet, which is Google's parent, along with Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, and Oracle — have been signing enormous long-term contracts to buy the specialized chips and servers that power artificial intelligence. They've also been leasing data centers in ways that, under accounting rules, don't get counted as debt on the books. But the obligation is still very real. And Nikkei's analysis finds these hidden liabilities have grown eightfold in four years. They now actually exceed these companies' officially reported debt, which stands at about one-point-three-five trillion dollars.

Meta is the most exposed. Its shadow debt runs to about four hundred and twenty billion dollars — nearly three times its reported liabilities. Now, why should you care about accounting footnotes? Because this is the entire AI boom in miniature. These companies are betting colossal sums that demand for AI will keep climbing. If it does, these contracts are just the cost of doing business. But if AI adoption disappoints — if those gleaming new data centers sit half-empty — then all that off-the-books spending becomes a very visible problem. Regulators and investors are starting to ask whether the true risk is being hidden in plain sight. It's worth watching closely, because so much of the global economy is now leaning on these bets.

Let's come home to Japan now, and a story that's smaller in scale but oddly gripping. Japan's Fair Trade Commission has filed charges against thirty-eight construction firms for rigging bids on large-scale apartment renovation projects. If you live in a Japanese condominium, you'll know that buildings are run by management boards made up of residents, and every so often they undertake big, expensive repair projects.

Here's the twist. The consultants those boards hired — the very people meant to keep costs down — were, according to Nikkei, colluding with the construction companies to inflate prices. The consultants pocketed commissions of five to ten percent of the total project cost, which pushed overall expenses up by around twenty percent. And it gets stranger. In some cases, fraudsters posing as ordinary residents actually infiltrated the management boards to steer contracts to chosen contractors. One scheme in Kanagawa was caught before completion and has led to criminal charges. In some proposals, prices ran ten million yen per apartment — about sixty-seven thousand dollars — above fair market value. The city of Yokohama is now offering free tools so boards can check quotes against real benchmarks. A reminder that trust, once it's engineered into a system, can be quietly exploited.

Now, over to Britain, where there's been a change at the top and it connects to a bigger story about the country's long economic drift. Sir Keir Starmer of the Labour Party took office as Prime Minister on the twentieth, after his party's win in May's general election. In his first address he promised what he called the greatest transformation in forty years — vowing to reverse the Thatcher-era policies of the eighties by bringing water, transport, and other utilities back under public control. Our own Prime Minister, Sanae Takaichi, posted congratulations, expressing hope to deepen the UK-Japan relationship.

But Nikkei pairs this with a sobering piece on what it calls Britain's "lost eighteen years." Real wage growth from 2008 to 2025 averaged just zero-point-two percent a year — third-worst in the G7. It took twelve years for real wages simply to climb back to where they were in early 2008. And young people are leaving: over a hundred and three thousand under-thirty-fives emigrated on a net basis last year. The paper profiles one Oxford graduate, Rufus Fox, who's now looking to Asia. The financial crisis, austerity, Brexit, weak investment — it all compounded. And the political danger is clear: the right-wing populist party Reform UK now leads in the polls. Starmer's promises will mean little if the economy doesn't turn.

Staying in Europe, Brussels has hit the Chinese e-commerce giant Alibaba with a record fine — five hundred and fifty million euros, about a billion dollars — for failing to keep illegal goods off its AliExpress platform. Under the EU's Digital Services Act, the Commission found that counterfeit products, unsafe toys, and dangerous cosmetics stayed listed for weeks even after being flagged. It's the largest such penalty yet. Alibaba says the fine is disproportionate and plans to appeal.

Now, a run of stories from Hungary that together paint a picture of real political turmoil. Following the change of government in May, Péter Szijjártó — the outgoing foreign minister and a close ally of former Prime Minister Viktor Orbán — has taken a senior job at the Chinese electric-vehicle maker BYD. And this raises alarm, because Szijjártó was the lead negotiator on Hungary's BYD partnership and would have handled sensitive EU diplomatic information. Chinese companies, by law, must share data with Beijing on national security matters. Add to that the revelation back in March of leaked calls with Russia's foreign minister Lavrov, in which Szijjártó discussed EU sanctions, and you can see why the new prime minister, Márton Márki-Zay, has used the word "treason" and ordered investigations.

At the same time, Hungary's parliament has amended the constitution to allow the early removal of President Tamás Sulyok, an Orbán-era appointee. Orbán called it an arbitrary abuse of power. So Hungary is a country visibly settling scores after a political handover — and doing it in ways that will unsettle its European partners.

A few more quickly from around the region. In Moscow, President Putin met North Korea's foreign minister, Choe Son-hui, thanking Pyongyang for its military support in Ukraine — including troops sent to help retake the Kursk region. They likely discussed a possible visit by Kim Jong-un. The two nations formalized what amounts to a Cold-War-style military alliance back in 2024, and it keeps deepening.

Closer to home, tensions flared again in the South China Sea. China's coast guard allegedly assaulted Philippine naval personnel with wooden batons at Ayungin Reef — you may know it as Second Thomas Shoal — injuring one sailor during a supply run. Manila condemned it as dangerous; Beijing said the Filipino account "inverts black and white." And Ukraine struck deep inside Russia, hitting two warehouses belonging to Wildberries, Russia's largest online retailer. Eight people were killed, more than eighty injured, with damages estimated around a hundred and four billion yen. Ukraine says the warehouses supplied drone components.

On the business front, a bit of good news from Japanese industry. Sumitomo Chemical plans to begin mass-producing a key material for all-solid-state batteries by fiscal 2028 — a halogen-based electrolyte that performs as well as existing options but is cheaper to manufacture and can reuse existing production equipment. If solid-state batteries are the next leap for electric vehicles, this is a meaningful step, and it's happening here.

And finally, the one that caught my eye. Honda is extending its joint venture with China's state-owned Guangzhou Automobile Group by a full decade, to 2038 — even as its sales in China have been collapsing. Think about that. A Japanese carmaker doubling down on a market where it's losing ground, betting the long game is still worth playing. It tells you just how hard it is to walk away from the world's largest auto market, even when the numbers are turning against you.
