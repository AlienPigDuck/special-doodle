Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Friday, the seventeenth of July, 2026, and I'm glad you're with me.

We begin this morning in an unexpected place: Poland. Seven&i Holdings — the Japanese company behind the 7-Eleven convenience stores you find on almost every corner in Japan — is making a big move into Europe. Nikkei reports this morning that the company is investing several billion euros in Jabłko Group, Poland's leading convenience store chain. That deal brings roughly ten thousand Polish stores into the fold.

Now, why does this matter? A couple of reasons. First, this is the first large-scale acquisition under Seven&i's new president, Stephen Daicus — and a new president's first big deal always tells you something about where they want to take the company. Daicus is signaling ambition abroad. Seven&i wants to grow from about 87,000 stores worldwide today to a hundred thousand by the 2030 financial year, and it's betting on Europe — a market Japanese retailers have largely left alone — to get there.

And there's a bigger story underneath this one. For years, the conversation around Seven&i was defensive. You may remember it was itself the target of a foreign takeover approach. Now it's on the front foot, pursuing an aggressive overseas strategy. That shift — from playing defense to playing offense — is worth watching, because it may signal a new confidence among Japan's retail giants about competing on the global stage.

Speaking of confidence, let's talk about money in people's pockets, because there's a milestone on the front page this morning. Japanese workers received an average summer bonus this year of just over one million yen — 1,044,168 yen, to be precise. That's up nearly four and a half percent from last year, and it's the first time the average has ever crossed the one-million-yen mark.

If you're not familiar, summer and winter bonuses are a huge part of Japanese compensation — often several months of salary, paid in two lump sums. So this isn't a small perk. It's a real read on the health of corporate Japan.

And what's driving it? In a word: artificial intelligence. Nikkei points to a boom in demand at semiconductor and manufacturing companies. Consider this — three years ago, 73 firms paid bonuses above a million yen. This year, that number is 150. It's more than doubled.

You can see the AI story running right through the business pages. The AI consulting startup Ridge-i nearly tripled its bonuses, to 1.3 million yen, after landing major orders from SBI Holdings. The chip equipment maker SCREEN Holdings paid out 2.35 million yen after record profits from data center gear. And Minebea Mitsumi — which makes precision motors — saw bonuses jump nearly 29 percent, because everyone suddenly needs cooling fans for their data centers. Meanwhile, Taiwan's TSMC, the world's most important chipmaker, just raised its investment plans for the year to as much as 64 billion dollars. The AI buildout, in other words, is very real.

But — and here's where I want you to lean in — the markets are getting nervous. Nikkei reports that AI and semiconductor stock indices crashed eight percent in a single session. The chip company Kiokushia, whose stock had tripled in eighteen months, is now down 45 percent from its June highs. The broader semiconductor sector is off about twenty percent from its peak.

So sit with that contradiction for a moment. The earnings are excellent. TSMC and ASML are doing beautifully. And yet investors are pulling back hard. The concern is that valuations simply ran too hot, too fast — and that heavily leveraged retail investors, especially in Korea, are amplifying the swings on the way down. This isn't a story about weak business. It's a story about a market that got ahead of itself. Worth keeping an eye on.

And here's an interesting counterpoint: ordinary Japanese savers are quietly moving in the opposite direction. Individual holdings of government bonds have surged to 20 trillion yen — the highest in twelve years. With yields now between one and two percent, those bonds finally look attractive next to bank deposits that pay almost nothing. After years of near-zero rates, Japanese households are rediscovering the humble government bond.

Let's turn to politics, where the government is making a series of consequential moves. First, there's a plan to make life harder for activist shareholders — raising the threshold to call an emergency shareholder meeting from three percent of voting rights to five. That brings Japan closer to European norms, and it reflects an ongoing tension: how much shareholder pressure is healthy discipline, and how much is disruptive interference?

The Diet is also finalizing something historic — the first substantive revision to the Imperial Household Law since 1947. The amendment would allow female members of the imperial family to keep their status after marriage, and would allow male-line descendants from former imperial branches to be restored. This touches one of Japan's most sensitive questions: the shrinking imperial family and the future of succession.

There's also a bill moving forward that would criminalize desecration of the national flag, with penalties up to two years in prison. Opposition lawmakers worry the definitions are too vague and could chill free expression — a debate that will feel familiar to listeners in many countries.

And a sadder note from the front page. Shinichi Tonegawa has died at the age of 86. He won the 1987 Nobel Prize in Medicine for discovering how our immune cells generate the astonishing diversity of antibodies that protect us. He later reinvented himself as a neuroscientist, studying memory and the brain. A rare mind — curious to the end.

Abroad, briefly: Germany's Mertz government is committing to one trillion euros in new borrowing to nearly triple defense spending by 2029 — a remarkable shift for a country long allergic to military expansion. Tensions are flaring again in the Persian Gulf, with Iran threatening the Strait of Hormuz. And Taiwan has launched its own homegrown AI language model, called TAIDE, trained on Taiwanese data — framing artificial intelligence as a matter of cultural sovereignty against Chinese influence.

And now, the one that caught my eye — buried deep in the paper, but strategically enormous. Japan's Rokkasho reprocessing plant, in the north, has just been delayed again. This is the 28th delay. Let me say that again: the twenty-eighth. New regulatory requirements mean more waste has to be processed before the plant can even open, and it may now miss its target this financial year.

Why should you care about a plant most people have never heard of? Because Rokkasho is the linchpin of Japan's entire "nuclear fuel cycle" — the plan to recycle spent nuclear fuel rather than simply bury it. After Fukushima, Japan's energy security became a national obsession. And yet this piece of critical infrastructure has been stalled for six decades. Twenty-eight delays. It rarely makes headlines, but it quietly shapes everything from Japan's climate goals to regional evacuation planning. A story of grand ambition and stubborn reality.
