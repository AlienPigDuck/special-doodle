Let's begin with electricity — because that turns out to be the story sitting underneath almost everything else in the paper this morning.

Nikkei reports that eight of Japan's major power companies, including Tokyo Electric Power, are racing to rebuild the country's electrical grid to feed the enormous appetite of artificial intelligence. Data centers — the vast warehouses of computers that make AI work — consume staggering amounts of power. So these utilities are expanding transmission infrastructure at thirty locations across the country. The scale here is worth pausing on. They're planning to add fifteen million kilowatts of capacity by the early 2030s. That's the equivalent of fifteen nuclear reactors. And they're deliberately building faster than demand actually requires — roughly twice as fast — because they don't want to be the bottleneck that sends AI investment elsewhere.

The price tag is 4.8 trillion yen a year, nationwide. And the context is a global arms race. Meta, in the United States, is pouring something like a hundred trillion yen into data centers. Japan doesn't want to watch that investment flow past. So think of this as the physical foundation — the concrete and copper — beneath the AI boom you keep hearing about.

And speaking of that boom, Jensen Huang, the chief executive of Nvidia, is in Japan this week making his pitch. Nvidia now earns more than three hundred billion dollars — about forty-nine trillion yen — from what it calls "sovereign AI." That's the idea that nations, not just companies, should build their own AI capacity. Huang met with a Japanese consortium called Noetra, backed by SoftBank, NEC, Honda, and Sony, and showed off Nvidia's latest chip, the H200. Interestingly, the U.S. Commerce Department has confirmed limited exports of that same chip to China are now underway under license — a small crack in the wall of restrictions.

Now, let's turn to Japan's automakers, because they're making a quiet but profound bet. Toyota, Honda, and Nissan are shifting away from simply selling you a car — and toward selling you an ongoing relationship with it. The model is software subscriptions. Tesla leads here: it's turned its autonomous driving features into a ninety-nine-dollar-a-month subscription, with a hundred and twenty-eight million users. In China, BYD and Huawei are doing the same with paid driving add-ons.

Here's where Japan has an advantage. Toyota has a hundred and fifty million vehicles on the road worldwide. Tesla has nine million. BYD, nineteen. If you can sell software to cars already out there, that installed base becomes a goldmine. Toyota is targeting two trillion yen in revenue that has nothing to do with metal and rubber. The car, increasingly, is becoming a smartphone with wheels.

Now, a cautionary tale about what happens when the digital systems that run modern life go down. Nichirei — Japan's largest cold-storage logistics company — was hit by a cyberattack on Sunday. It had to halt warehouse operations entirely, and personal data was exposed. And because Nichirei sits at the center of the country's frozen food supply chain, the disruption rippled outward — hitting KFC, Kura Sushi, and ice cream makers. The company expects to resume service tomorrow. But it's a reminder of how a single point of failure can seize up an entire industry.

Let me turn to the Bank of Japan, where some old ghosts have resurfaced. Newly released minutes from early 2016 reveal that the central bank's own board members sharply criticized the decision that January to introduce negative interest rates. They called it "hasty" and "underdeveloped." One member, Kiyohide Ueda, objected on governance grounds. Now, why does this matter a decade later? Because the current governor, Kazuo Ueda — no relation — is under pressure to show that today's Bank of Japan deliberates transparently and carefully. These minutes are an uncomfortable reminder of a time when, by the board's own account, it didn't.

To politics now. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi told parliament that the proposed cut to the food consumption tax — from eight percent down to just one percent — won't be decided this month after all. It's been pushed to early August and handed to an all-party task force. Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama warned that dragging this out creates uncertainty that could unsettle markets and interest rates.

Two other moves in Tokyo caught my attention. Defense Minister Shinjiro Koizumi held the first-ever summit between the Defense Ministry and Japan's banks, urging them to fund defense startups in dual-use technology and semiconductors. The banks are wary — partly because of their exposure to China, partly because defense contracts tend to be one-off and low-margin.

And the Diet approved a "vice-capital" system — a plan to decentralize government away from Tokyo, with Osaka, Nagoya, Fukuoka, and Sapporo all competing for the designation. Hold that thought, because it connects to something happening abroad.

Internationally, the Middle East remains tense. The Trump administration re-imposed a naval blockade on Iranian ports on Monday, then abruptly reversed a proposed twenty-percent transit fee on the Strait of Hormuz after Gulf allies pushed back. Trump is now seeking "investment-for-security" pledges from Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, Bahrain, and Kuwait. Oil held near eighty dollars a barrel. For Japan — which imports nearly all its energy — Trump's threats against nations buying Russian oil could force some awkward alignment decisions.

That turbulence is showing up in China's numbers. Second-quarter growth came in at 4.3 percent, down from five percent in the first quarter. Consumption barely moved. Auto sales fell nearly thirteen percent after Beijing cut its EV tax breaks. The World Bank now expects China to fall short of its own growth target this year. Economists are using an uncomfortable word: stagflation — weak growth with rising costs.

Two quick notes from Europe. In Ukraine, the cabinet has resigned amid tension between the presidency and parliament over anti-corruption and defense reforms — which could slow the country's path toward EU membership. And in Bulgaria, a new pro-Russia prime minister has pulled the country out of the Ukraine support coalition. Meanwhile in Britain, incoming Prime Minister Keir Starmer plans a "Number 10 North" office in Manchester to spread power beyond London. Which brings us full circle — because that's precisely the same instinct behind Japan's vice-capital plan. It seems the whole democratic world is wrestling with the same question: how do you stop everything piling up in the capital?

A few business notes before we close. In South Korea, SK Group chairman Chey Tae-won saw his stock holdings nearly triple in three months — from 3.9 trillion won to 10.8 trillion — riding a rally in SK Hynix memory chips. He's now the country's third-richest tycoon. And China's AI startup DeepSeek is preparing an IPO that could value it at seventy-one billion dollars, as it takes on Alibaba and ByteDance in cost-efficient AI.

And now, the one that caught my eye — from deeper in the paper, and from an unexpected corner. Thailand's largest hospital operator, Bangkok Dusit Medical Services, is getting into luxury real estate. It's building a hundred-and-forty-billion-yen complex near Lumphini Park in Bangkok — two hundred thousand square meters of high-end residences and wellness facilities, developed with Singapore's Capella Hotels. The target: Asia's wealthy, aging population.

What I find fascinating is the logic. A hospital company is betting that the future isn't in treating illness — it's in preventing it, and in selling a lifestyle around longevity. The Southeast Asian wellness market is projected to grow from fifty-three billion dollars today to three hundred and thirty-two billion by 2034. A hospital deciding its best growth isn't in hospitals — that tells you something about where Asia thinks it's headed.
