Our lead story this morning is about something most of us never think about — until suddenly we have to. Rare metals. These are the obscure-sounding elements that make modern life possible: tungsten, tellurium, bismuth, molybdenum, indium. They go into everything from smartphones to fighter jets to solar panels. And according to the Nikkei this morning, China is tightening its grip on these materials in a deliberate, two-handed strategy.

Here's how it works. On one hand, China is buying up rare metals aggressively from countries it considers friendly. Between January and May of this year, Chinese imports of these targeted metals jumped 60 percent compared to last year — to 350,000 tons. And imports from allied nations like Myanmar and North Korea actually tripled. So China is hoarding supply.

On the other hand, China is choking off exports to the West. Back in February, Beijing imposed export restrictions on those five metals I just mentioned. The result has been global shortages and price spikes.

Takeshi Okabe, a researcher at the University of Tokyo, put it plainly. He says China is using rare metals as — and I'm quoting — "diplomatic leverage and coercion cards against the West." In other words, these aren't just commodities. They're bargaining chips. And Japan, the United States, and Europe are now scrambling to find alternative sources, the way they once had to diversify away from Middle Eastern oil. This is a quiet but very serious story, and it's one we'll be returning to.

Now, before we leave China — there's a related thread further into the paper that ties together with our story at the end, so keep that rare metals strategy in your mind.

Let's turn to the front page here in Japan, where two big domestic stories stand out.

First, finance. Three of Japan's megabanks — Mitsubishi UFJ, Mitsui Sumitomo, and Mizuho — are teaming up with 25 other firms, including regional banks and tech giants NTT Data and NEC, to build something genuinely new: an autonomous, AI-driven personal asset management system. Launching in July, with full commercial deployment targeted for 2028, it would use AI chatbots to walk ordinary investors through choosing a mutual fund and actually completing the purchase. The idea is to democratize wealth management for busy, time-strapped workers who don't have a private banker. Nikkei calls this Japan's first large-scale, cross-company AI initiative in the financial sector. They're still working through the regulatory questions — anti-money-laundering, consumer protection — with pilot testing planned for 2027.

The second big domestic story is about your grocery bill. A ruling coalition taskforce has proposed cutting the consumption tax on food to just one percent — temporarily, for two years, starting April 2027. And by adding a one percent cash payout on top, they're aiming for what they call an "effective zero" rate. Now, the tricky part is paying for it. This costs about five trillion yen a year, and the coalition wants to fund it through subsidy reviews and other non-tax revenue — pointedly avoiding new deficit bonds. There's resistance, though, especially from regional economies worried about the administrative headache of constantly changing tax rates.

Now, defense — a theme running right through today's paper. Kawasaki Heavy Industries has signed a memorandum of understanding with Airbus Defense and Space in Europe. The plan is to fit Kawasaki's anti-submarine surveillance technology — sonar, magnetic detection — onto Europe's Eurodrone program. This is Japan's first major foreign defense drone partnership, and it points to a deepening European-Japanese defense relationship.

Staying with defense and politics: Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi has pushed back Japan's target for a 1,500-yen minimum wage from the 2020s to the early 2030s — easing pressure on small businesses while still aiming for annual raises above three percent. The government is also sweetening retirement terms for Self-Defense officers, expanding transition pay to the equivalent of nine months' salary by 2028. Why? Recruitment. Japan is facing a real military personnel crisis. And in a notable rebrand, the Air Self-Defense Force will become the "Aerospace Self-Defense Force," with new space surveillance capabilities and an elevated Space Operations Command.

One more from the home front, and it's a big one historically: the Imperial Household Law. Japan is moving to allow male-line descendants from former branches of the imperial family to retain succession rights — if they're adopted as full members. This is part of the long, careful effort to address a genuine worry: a shrinking imperial family and an uncertain line of succession.

Now to markets — and it was an ugly day in Tokyo. The Nikkei index plunged 3,005 points — that's four percent — its third-largest single-day point drop on record. The trigger was a report that OpenAI is postponing its IPO, which spooked investors in AI and semiconductor stocks. SoftBank Group, deeply tied to the AI boom, tumbled 13 percent in a single session.
