Our lead story this morning is about the price of war — and I mean that quite literally. The Nikkei reports that President Trump's hundred-day military campaign against Iran has cost the U.S. Department of Defense at least four-point-seven trillion yen — that's about twenty-nine billion dollars — by the middle of May. And when you fold in allied support, the total reaches roughly twelve trillion yen, or seventy-two billion dollars, in just the first two months. To put that in perspective for our listeners here in Japan: that figure exceeds Japan's entire annual defense budget. The whole thing.

And here's the uncomfortable part. Despite the killing of Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei, Iran did not surrender — which is apparently what the Trump administration expected. Instead, Tehran shifted to what military analysts call asymmetric warfare: speedboats, drones, low-cost weapons that are cheap to deploy but expensive to defend against. The result, according to the Nikkei, is that the United States has now depleted half of its air defense inventory — the very stockpile it needs for deterrence in the Indo-Pacific. That should concern everyone in this region.

Politically, it's not going well either. Sixty-three percent of unaffiliated American voters now view the war as against U.S. interests. And strategically, Iran still controls the Strait of Hormuz — that narrow waterway through which a huge share of the world's oil passes. Tehran holds real leverage over global energy supplies. So an enormous expenditure, the death of a head of state, and yet a result that falls well short of what the administration promised.

And this connects to a smaller but telling story from the international pages. Experts tell the Nikkei that clearing the Iranian mines from the Strait of Hormuz — to meet a thirty-day ceasefire deadline — will actually take one to two months. The mines were laid in uncertain positions, the waters are harsh, and the work requires careful international naval coordination. And here's a point of quiet national pride: that effort would lean heavily on Japan's minesweeping capability, which is among the most advanced in the world. It's a reminder that Japan's role in global security isn't always about firepower. Sometimes it's about the patient, dangerous work of making the sea safe again.

Closer to home, the security picture is tense in other ways too. On June twenty-seventh, Chinese and Russian air forces conducted a joint strategic flight near Japan — fighters and bombers transiting the Miyako Strait, the gap between Okinawa's main island and Miyako Island. Japan's Air Self-Defense Force scrambled fighters in response and filed diplomatic protests. These joint patrols have become a recurring message from Beijing and Moscow, and Tokyo reads them exactly as they're intended.

Against that backdrop, there's movement on a relationship that matters. Defense Minister Shinjiro Koizumi met his South Korean counterpart, Ahn Kyou-baek, in Seoul, and the two agreed to expand joint training and equipment cooperation. Japan and South Korea have a complicated history, and defense ties between them have often been fragile. But with uncertainty about American commitments and real threats in the region, the two neighbors are drawing closer. That's significant.

Now, let's turn to domestic politics, because Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi is having a remarkable run. A Nikkei–TV Tokyo poll taken over the weekend puts her cabinet's approval at sixty-eight percent — up two points. And here's the headline within the headline: among voters thirty-nine and under, support reaches seventy-eight percent. Her administration has now held above sixty percent for nine straight months — and the Nikkei notes that's unprecedented under this survey's methodology going back to 2002.

What's driving it? Well, part of the conversation is a proposed temporary cut to the food consumption tax — one percent — paired with cash transfers to low-income households. The public is split: forty-nine percent support it, forty-five percent oppose. But look at the age gap. Among voters under forty, support climbs to sixty-two percent. Among those sixty and over, it drops to forty-six percent. That generational divide is becoming the defining feature of Takaichi's coalition — she's governing with the young.

Let's move to business, where there's a striking theme this morning: how Japanese companies are rethinking the way they treat their workers and their suppliers.

Start with Fast Retailing — the parent of Uniqlo. The Nikkei reports the company is tightening human rights audits across more than seven hundred factories worldwide, going beyond standard compliance to country-specific monitoring of labor violations, child labor, and wage theft. This is partly about EU rules taking full effect in 2029, requiring corporate human rights due diligence. But there's history here too. Back in 2021, Fast Retailing was caught up in a dispute over cotton sourced from Xinjiang, which led to U.S. import restrictions. So this is a company that has learned, the hard way, that supply chains are now a reputational frontline.

And on the labor side, a generational shift. Hoshino Resorts — the luxury hospitality group — is breaking with one of the most rigid traditions in Japanese corporate life: the single spring hiring cohort. You may know that Japanese firms typically recruit new graduates all at once, every April. Hoshino is now letting students choose from multiple entry dates — April, June, or October — to attract, in their words, less conformist talent.

Meanwhile, Nitto Denko, the materials company, is offering young employees fifty thousand yen a month for up to four years — that's two-point-four million yen total — to soften the blow of being transferred to another city. Workers, it turns out, increasingly resist being uprooted, and competitors like Sumitomo Life and Taisei are offering even more. The era of moving employees around like furniture is, slowly, ending.

Two quick ones from the cosmetics world, where artificial intelligence is moving from the lab into the bottle. Shiseido has launched its first commercial product designed with a proprietary AI called Voyager — a mist-type sunscreen, born from a system that's studied more than five hundred thousand past formulations. And L'Oréal, the world's largest cosmetics company, is teaming up with Nvidia to speed up ingredient discovery, with claims of identifying promising compounds a hundred times faster. The age of AI-formulated beauty is, apparently, here.

And before we go, the one that caught my eye — and this one is extraordinary. Buried deep in the Features section, the Nikkei has published an investigative report on a fentanyl trafficking network that exploited Japan's weak oversight of cryptocurrency and internet domains. According to the reporting, a Chinese pharmaceutical company called Hubei Amarvel Biotech, operating through a Japanese subsidiary named Firsky, used Japan's lax dot-jp domain registration rules and gaps in cryptocurrency oversight to launder hundreds of millions of dollars in fentanyl proceeds. The network is connected to a man the U.S. Justice Department calls a drug kingpin — Ye Chuanfa — who carries a five-million-dollar bounty. The Nikkei traces four cryptocurrency accounts that moved more than seven hundred thousand dollars in Bitcoin.

What makes this story so unsettling is the role Japan plays in it — not as a target, but as a convenient back door. A country known for its order and its rules, exploited precisely because some of those rules haven't kept pace with the digital age. It's a reminder that the global drug trade now flows through domain registrars and crypto wallets as much as through ports and borders. I suspect we'll hear more about this one.
