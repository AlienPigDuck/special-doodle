Let's begin.

Our lead story this morning is the beginning of a five-part investigative series in the Nikkei called "Superintelligence," and it's about what happens when two of the most powerful technologies of our age collide — artificial intelligence and quantum computing.

Now, if you've followed either of these fields, you know they've mostly developed on separate tracks. Quantum computers promise to solve problems that would take today's fastest supercomputers billions of years — but they're famously fragile, prone to errors that make them unreliable. What the Nikkei reports is that researchers have found a way to use AI to correct those quantum errors in real time. That's the breakthrough. And here in Japan, the National Institute of Advanced Industrial Science and Technology — that's AIST, the country's flagship public research body — is partnering with Nvidia to build these hybrid quantum-AI systems. Nvidia's chief executive, Jensen Huang, is said to be personally invested in the project.

So why does this matter beyond the laboratory? Two reasons. First, the economic promise is enormous. Second — and this is the part that keeps national security officials up at night — there's something researchers call "Q-Day." That's the moment when a quantum computer becomes powerful enough to break the encryption that protects, well, almost everything. Your bank account. Government secrets. Military communications. The Trump administration in the United States has committed two billion dollars to quantum research, and China has declared it a strategic priority.

And here's the pressure point for Japan. The AIST-Nvidia partnership is valuable, but Nikkei's reporting carries a warning from Japanese researchers: relying on international partnerships alone is risky. Without rapid domestic investment, they say, Japan could fall behind in what may be the defining technology of the century. That tension — between collaboration and self-reliance — is a theme you're going to hear again and again in today's paper.

Because staying on the front page, Japan just pulled off something genuinely remarkable in space. On June 5th, the JAXA space agency used the Hayabusa2 spacecraft to perform a high-precision flyby of an asteroid called Trifine. And when I say precision, I mean it. The probe was traveling at eighteen thousand kilometers an hour and passed within four to six hundred meters of the asteroid's surface — autonomously, guiding itself.

This is only the second time in history anyone has done this. NASA managed it once, back in 2022, with its DART probe. So Japan becomes just the second nation to master a capability that could one day protect all of us — the ability to steer a spacecraft precisely enough to deflect an asteroid on a collision course with Earth. Now, an important caveat: this was a demonstration of the guidance, not an actual deflection. The mechanism for nudging an asteroid off course remains untested. But the Nikkei calls this world-class technology, and it's hard to argue.

From a triumph in the skies to a setback closer to home. Nikkei reports that the British oil major BP is withdrawing from Japan's largest offshore wind project, in Yamagata Prefecture. BP held roughly a quarter of the consortium led by the trading house Marubeni — a stake comparable to those of Kansai Electric Power and Tokyo Gas. The consortium will carry on without them, but this is the first major withdrawal among Japan's nine government-led offshore wind sites, and it matters.

Why? Because it's a warning sign. Inflation and rising costs have eroded overseas investor confidence in Japanese renewables. BP blames the difficulty of folding the project into its new JERA Nex bp joint venture — that's a partnership with JERA, Japan's largest thermal power company. But the broader message is that Japan's ambitious climate targets depend on foreign capital that's suddenly harder to attract.

And that theme of energy vulnerability runs straight through today's international coverage, so let's stay with it.

The most consequential story here is Iran's de facto closure of the Strait of Hormuz, following missile exchanges with Israel. Twenty percent of the world's petroleum and liquefied natural gas passes through that narrow waterway. And Nikkei's analysis is sobering: it argues this blockade is no longer a temporary crisis but a permanent feature of Middle Eastern geopolitics. Oil has pushed above a hundred dollars a barrel.

But here's the deeper point the paper draws out. Hormuz is just one chokepoint. There's also the Malacca Strait — which carries thirty percent of the world's oil shipments — and the Taiwan Strait. Both face similar closure risks. For an island nation like Japan, which imports nearly all of its energy, the assumption of free passage through these narrow waters has underpinned the entire economy. Nikkei's warning is that this assumption may no longer hold, and that supply chains will have to be fundamentally rethought.

Which brings us naturally to rare earths. JOGMEC — that's Japan's oil, gas and metals corporation — has confirmed substantial rare earth deposits in Greenland's Kujalleq region. Rare earths are the seventeen elements essential to electric vehicles and high-tech manufacturing, and China controls seventy percent of global output. In 2025, China introduced export restrictions. So Greenland, with an estimated thirty-six million tons of undiscovered resources, has become a focal point for Japan, the United States, and Europe to diversify away from Beijing. The challenge that remains, as always, is processing — mining the ore is one thing, refining it is another.

Elsewhere on the international pages, President Trump held phone calls with both Vladimir Putin and Volodymyr Zelenskyy about Ukraine. Putin asserted Russian military superiority; Trump signaled willingness to send envoys to Moscow to facilitate talks. The calls revealed fundamental disagreements about ceasefire terms.

And America marked its 250th birthday on the Fourth of July. Nikkei's account is striking — some 375,000 attendees, intense heat causing heat-stroke cases, thunderstorms forcing evacuations, and a record 850,000 fireworks. President Trump delivered his keynote after midnight, using the occasion to promote his election legislation and to call the Democratic Party "communist," while protesters gathered downtown against his immigration policies. The paper's framing is pointed: even a ceremonial occasion has become a vehicle for partisan messaging.

Now, at home, one story deserves your attention. The cabinet has approved revisions to Japan's Imperial Household Law. Under the proposal, female members of the imperial family could remain in the household after they marry — though their husbands and children would not gain imperial status. It would also allow the adoption of male descendants from defunct branches of the family to secure the line of succession.

This has ignited real debate. Conservative scholars call male-line succession the very pillar of the imperial institution. Reformists counter that keeping women in the family while excluding their husbands and children is impractical and discriminatory — and that the male-line-only system simply cannot sustain itself. It's a quiet story, but it touches something profound about how Japan understands continuity and tradition.
