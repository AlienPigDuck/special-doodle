Think of it as Japan's answer to the Wall Street Journal or the Financial Times, but with a reach and an influence inside corporate Japan that's genuinely hard to overstate. For years the story was deflation — prices that wouldn't rise, wages that wouldn't move, a central bank pinning interest rates near zero and below. That story has been changing. The Bank of Japan has been slowly, carefully stepping away from the extraordinary policies it held onto longer than almost any central bank in the world. Every meeting, every hint from the governor, every inflation figure gets parsed for what it means about the pace of that shift. The yen itself is a story that never really goes away. A weaker yen makes Japanese exports — the cars, the machinery, the electronics — cheaper and more competitive abroad, which the big manufacturers like. But it also makes everything Japan imports more expensive, and Japan imports a great deal, including most of its energy. So a weak yen quietly raises the cost of living for ordinary households. That tension — good for exporters, hard on families — runs underneath an enormous amount of Japanese economic news, and it's worth holding in your mind as a lens.

Then there's the demographic question, which is really the deepest current of all. Japan is aging faster than almost any society in human history, and its population is shrinking. That single fact touches nearly everything: the labor shortage that has companies scrambling for workers and, increasingly, opening the door to more foreign labor than Japan has traditionally welcomed; the strain on the pension and healthcare systems; the emptying-out of rural towns; and the enormous national interest in robotics and automation as a way to do more with fewer hands. When you read about a Japanese company investing heavily in factory automation, or a town offering money to families to move in, you're really reading a chapter of the demographic story.
