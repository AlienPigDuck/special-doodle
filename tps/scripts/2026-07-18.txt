Let's begin.

Our lead story is one of the most significant changes to Japan's monarchy in the postwar era. Yesterday, July seventeenth, the Upper House of Japan's parliament approved a revised Imperial Household Law. Now, this is the legal framework that governs the imperial family — who counts as a member, who can inherit the throne, and what happens when someone marries. And it hasn't been meaningfully overhauled since the current system was set up after the Second World War.

So what changes? Two big things. First, female members of the imperial family will now be able to keep their royal status even after they marry. Under the old rules, a princess who married a commoner simply left the family — which, in a shrinking imperial household, meant losing member after member. Second, and more controversially, the law now allows male descendants from former imperial branches to be adopted as successors.

Here's the context that makes this so charged. Japan's imperial succession runs through the male line only, and the family is running short of eligible male heirs. This is not an abstract worry — it's a genuine demographic problem for one of the world's oldest continuous monarchies. So the pressure to find a solution has been building for years.

But here's the friction. Matters concerning the imperial family have traditionally been handled by cross-party consensus — the idea being that the monarchy belongs above ordinary politics, and its rules shouldn't be forced through by one side. This time, that consensus broke. Five political parties voted against the bill. Critics say the government went further than what had been agreed — specifically by granting succession rights to the *descendants* of adopted sons, not just the adopted sons themselves. And their argument is subtle but important: if the rules governing the throne are passed over the objections of a large chunk of parliament, does that weaken the very legitimacy the monarchy is supposed to embody?

Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi struck a careful note, saying the government would make, in her words, "full preparations." But this passage tells you something about her administration more broadly — and that theme runs through several stories this morning.

Because look at the Diet itself. Nikkei reports the Lower House has extended this special parliamentary session through July twenty-fifth, pushing back several bills — including measures on a so-called deputy-capital framework and changes to vaccine policy. Why the delay? It comes down to a structural awkwardness. Takaichi's coalition holds more than two-thirds of the Lower House — a commanding majority — but it's in the *minority* in the Upper House. So the government can pass things in one chamber and then hit a wall in the other. That mismatch is exposing real coordination problems, and it's shaping what does and doesn't get done.

Meanwhile, the government is wrestling with a couple of consequential economic decisions. Nikkei says a high-level fiscal council aims to decide by early August whether to cut the consumption tax on food — Japan's version of a sales tax — and pair that with new welfare payments for middle-income workers. That's a direct response to the cost-of-living squeeze many households are feeling. And separately, the government is going out of its way to reassure everyone that it will respect the independence of the Bank of Japan, even putting explicit language to that effect in its economic guidelines. That matters because markets have been nervous about political interference in monetary policy — so this is Takaichi's team saying, clearly, hands off the central bank.

Now let's turn to the markets, because it was a bruising day. The Nikkei Average — the benchmark index — fell four percent, closing at sixty-four thousand one hundred forty-one. That's its lowest in a month. The trigger? A deflating bubble in AI and semiconductor stocks. The memory-chip maker Kioxia Holdings plunged sixteen percent, driven in part by margin calls — that's when investors who borrowed money to buy shares are forced to sell as prices drop, which pushes prices down further. And the big debate now is whether this is just profit-taking after a long run-up, or something deeper — a fundamental rethink of how much these AI companies are really worth.

Interestingly, the money isn't leaving entirely. Nikkei reports it's rotating into REITs — real estate investment trusts — which are being eyed for dividend yields around five percent and their ability to hold up in a higher interest-rate world. When AI looks shaky, in other words, bricks and mortar start looking attractive again.

There's a global thread running through the markets too, and it's about geopolitics and energy. Asian prices for liquefied natural gas — LNG — jumped to a four-month high. The cause is escalating tension in the Middle East. Iran's Revolutionary Guards are threatening not just oil tankers in the Strait of Hormuz but also undersea communications cables — the physical fiber-optic lines that carry the internet across the seabed. It's a reminder of how much invisible infrastructure our daily lives depend on. And it's pushing companies to diversify. Nikkei notes that an Australian shale gas project backed by Japan's INPEX begins production in September, at 1.1 million cubic meters a day — part of a broader effort to reduce reliance on Middle Eastern supply.

A few more quick items from abroad. In Britain, incoming Prime Minister Andy Burnham is signaling a "big government" approach — more spending — which has markets jittery about debt and inflation. And yet the pound surged to an eighteen-year high against the yen, after Burnham appointed a fiscal-hawk finance minister. Markets, it seems, decided the grown-up in the budget office outweighs the spending talk. And China has launched a World AI Cooperation Organization with twenty-nine nations, with Xi Jinping framing it as protecting the interests of the Global South — clearly a counterweight to US-led restrictions on technology.

Speaking of the US — and this one hits close to home for Japan — the Trump administration is shortening student visa validity to a maximum of four years. Japanese universities and academics are worried about what it means for recruitment and research partnerships. And in a piece of news that will overlap with Tokyo's own trading day, the US Securities and Exchange Commission has approved extended trading hours starting December sixth — nearly twenty-three hours a day. The concern in Japan? That companies here might skip the domestic market and list directly in America, hollowing out Tokyo's capital markets.

And now, the one that caught my eye — buried, as it so often is, deep in the business pages. Nikkei reports on an unfolding scandal in the insurance industry: systematic evasion of social insurance contributions. Here's how it worked. Insurance sales brokerages allegedly split employee wages in two, in order to shrink the payroll on paper — the number that determines how much the employer has to pay into pension and healthcare funds. And apparently, labor brokers were selling *templates* for how to do this. Think about that. It's not one bad actor; it's an off-the-shelf product for cheating the system.

Why does this deserve more than a buried paragraph? Because Japan's pension and healthcare systems are already straining under an aging population. Every yen that quietly disappears from those funds makes the arithmetic worse. This is white-collar fraud, yes — but it's also a story about the country's fiscal future. I hope it gets the sustained attention it deserves.
