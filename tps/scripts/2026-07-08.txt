Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Wednesday, the eighth of July, 2026, and I'm glad you're here.

We begin this morning with a decision nearly a decade in the making — one that finally clears the way for what may be the most ambitious infrastructure project in Japan today.

The governor of Shizuoka Prefecture, Suzuki Yasutomo, has approved construction of the Maglev line — the Central Shinkansen — through his prefecture. And with that single signature yesterday, the last political obstacle to Japan's magnetic levitation railway megaproject has been removed.

Now, to understand why this matters, you have to understand what's been stuck. The Maglev is a train that floats above the track using magnets, running at speeds that would cut the trip between Tokyo and Nagoya from ninety minutes to just forty. But since 2017, one stretch — a little under nine kilometers through Shizuoka — has been frozen. The previous governor blocked it, worried the tunneling would drain water from local rivers. It was a genuine environmental concern, and it held up the entire line.

That nine-kilometer section is also the hardest to build — engineers expect it to take a full decade on its own. So the Nikkei reports that the Tokyo-to-Nagoya leg won't open until 2036 or later. That's nine years behind the original schedule. JR East will sign the environmental protection agreement on the eighteenth and start construction by year's end.

Here's the vision behind all this. Prime Minister Takaichi sees the Maglev as the spine of a single, connected economic megazone — Tokyo, Nagoya, and eventually Osaka, sixty-six million people and three hundred and thirty trillion yen of economic activity, all within easy reach of one another. Part growth engine, part disaster insurance: if one region is hit by an earthquake, the others stay connected. After nine years of stalemate, the shovels are finally going in the ground.

Now, let's turn to something quieter but genuinely unsettling — the growing evidence of cyber-vulnerability inside Japan's public institutions.

Mie Prefecture announced yesterday that it found forty-seven virus-infected USB devices across its public agencies. They tested roughly ten thousand of them. Eighteen carried previously unknown malware designed to steal information. The rest were older email viruses left behind by botched data transfers. Four of the infected devices were personal — brought in without authorization.

Why did Mie go looking in the first place? Because a China-linked virus had turned up in Japan's Self-Defense Forces systems. That set off alarms, and Mie decided to check its own house. No leaks have been confirmed, and the prefecture is now banning personal USB drives and installing dedicated scanning terminals. It's a small story from one prefecture — but it hints at how widely these threats may be lurking, unexamined, across the country.

And there's a private-sector response to exactly this kind of worry. NTT Data says it will roll out AI-powered cybersecurity services by late July, aimed at regional banks and government clients — using artificial intelligence to hunt for vulnerabilities before an attacker does. In a country where the Mie discovery is probably not unique, that's a market with room to grow.

Over in politics, the Takaichi government is doing some housekeeping on its priorities. The Prime Minister and Yoshimura Hiroshi, who leads the Japan Restoration Party, agreed yesterday to postpone a plan to cut the number of Lower House seats. That reduction is a signature Restoration goal, but it's been blocked by the opposition. So it moves to the autumn session, while the coalition focuses first on a bill to create a backup capital city and on reforms to imperial succession. In coalition politics, this is the art of choosing your battles.

The government also reached two compromises on the safety net. On elderly health care, they agreed to raise co-payments "based on ability to pay" — but crucially, without committing to any specific number, deferring the hard decisions to next fiscal year. And on pensions, they'll begin talks this summer to extend workplace insurance to people juggling multiple jobs and part-time workers — a nod to how much Japanese work has changed since these systems were designed.

Let's step out into the world, because there's a great deal moving.

The headline from Asia's tech sector is Samsung. The Korean giant's operating profit for April through June came in at nearly nine trillion yen — almost twenty times what it earned a year ago. The engine is artificial intelligence: data centers are devouring memory chips, and prices for DRAM and the high-bandwidth memory that AI depends on have hit record highs. There's even a word for it now — "chipflation." But it's not all celebration. Samsung is facing a U.S. antitrust lawsuit alleging it restricted DRAM supply. And meanwhile, the Nikkei reports that Chinese chipmakers CXMT and YMTC plan to double their production capacity by 2027 — betting that in a fragmented world, affordable chips will win out over national loyalties.

On defense, the picture is one of a rattled West. NATO members are meeting in Ankara, and the mood is tense. President Trump has been criticizing Italy's Giorgia Meloni over cooperation on Iran, and again dangling doubts about America's commitment to the alliance. In response, Europe is opening its wallet. Germany's cabinet has approved doubling its defense budget to roughly thirty-four trillion yen by 2030. And NATO members collectively pledged some thirteen trillion yen a year in support for Ukraine through 2027.

Ukraine, for its part, is on offense in a striking way. Nikkei reports that Ukrainian drones struck Russia's largest oil refinery, at Omsk — twenty-five hundred kilometers from Ukrainian territory. That's a remarkable reach. Kyiv says a forty-day campaign against Russian oil infrastructure has cut refining capacity by an estimated forty percent.

And two flashpoints worth watching for energy markets. In the Strait of Hormuz, Iran's Revolutionary Guards allegedly fired on two merchant ships, including a Qatari liquefied natural gas tanker — a dangerous move amid fragile ceasefire talks. And in the Pacific, China test-fired a submarine-launched ballistic missile that landed near Tuvalu's waters, drawing sharp rebukes from Australia, New Zealand, and the Philippines for threatening a region that prides itself on being nuclear-free.

Closer to home, a couple of business notes. Bridgestone launched air-free tires on autonomous shuttles in Shiga Prefecture today — puncture-proof, no inflation needed, the fruit of eighteen years of development, aimed at easing maintenance in rural transport. And the Fair Trade Commission has widened an investigation into six ice cream makers — Meiji, Lotte, Glico, and others — over suspected price-fixing. The wrinkle here is oddly human: retailers have long used ice cream as a loss-leader, keeping it at a hundred to a hundred and fifty yen to draw shoppers in. So the makers held prices flat — even as their costs rose twenty-five percent. Whether coordination crossed a legal line is what regulators now want to know.

And that brings me to the one that caught my eye this morning — a story that says a great deal about Japan quietly, without any drama.

The Japan Blood Products Organization announced a two-trillion-yen investment to double its production of immunoglobulin — a plasma-derived therapy that's genuinely life-saving for people with immune disorders. The goal is complete self-sufficiency in these critical products by 2040. Right now, Japan imports nearly sixty percent of what it needs.

I find this quietly compelling. In an era obsessed with chips and rare earths, here's a country deciding that its own citizens' blood — turned into medicine — is a strategic asset worth building factories for. It's supply-chain security, but of the most personal kind. A reminder that resilience isn't only about steel and silicon.
