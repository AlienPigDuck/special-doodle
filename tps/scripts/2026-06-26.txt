Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Friday, the twenty-sixth of June, and we have a story today that reads almost like a spy novel — except it's real, and it happened inside Japan's own military.

Let's start there. The Nikkei this morning reports that Japan's Ground Self-Defense Force — that's the army branch of the country's armed forces — unknowingly used Chinese-made USB drives infected with malware in its classified computer systems. And not for a few days. For nearly a year. From early 2024 all the way to February of 2025.

Here's what happened, according to internal documents the Nikkei obtained. Six infected USB drives were discovered at the Central Regional Command headquarters in Itami, in Hyogo Prefecture. Now, this is not some peripheral office. We're talking about what the military calls a "closed system" — the highly classified network that handles operational command orders and secret information, the kind of network that's supposed to be sealed off from the outside world precisely so this can't happen. Of roughly 480 computers connected to that system, more than 50 were exposed to the malware.

And here's the part that makes this so striking — the origin. The infection traces back to January 2024, to the disaster relief operations on the Noto Peninsula, after the earthquake there. The virus, which the Nikkei links to a Chinese hacker group, was embedded in counterfeit Chinese USB devices — fakes, sold widely on Amazon and Rakuten at half the normal price. Half price. Somebody bought cheap memory sticks, and that's how it got in.

Now, the GSDF says the system itself wasn't compromised — meaning, as far as they can tell, no classified information actually leaked out. That's the reassuring part. But step back and think about what this exposes. If half-price counterfeit USB drives, sold openly online, can find their way into the most sensitive corner of Japan's military, then they can find their way anywhere — into factories, into hospitals, into power plants. The vulnerability isn't really about the army. It's about a supply chain so flooded with cheap, compromised hardware that even careful institutions get caught. That's the lesson that should keep people up at night.

And it lands at a moment when Japan is thinking hard about security on every front — including energy. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi this morning announced a comprehensive energy security package, to be finalized by August. The trigger here is the recent turmoil in the Middle East, which exposed just how dependent Japan still is on Gulf oil. So the plan does several things at once: it diversifies crude oil sourcing beyond the Middle East, it accelerates the rebuilding of nuclear facilities, it promotes next-generation reactors, and it expands domestic renewables — including wind power and something called perovskite solar cells. Those are a flexible, thin, bendable kind of solar panel that Japan has been betting on, partly because the materials can be sourced domestically. In fact, in a related move, the government plans to relax safety standards for installing perovskite panels at industrial sites by fiscal 2026 — to let that flexibility actually be put to use.

Speaking of the Middle East, the international pages tell us how that crisis is winding down. The United States and Iran are expected to resume practical talks around June 30th, in Switzerland — the first substantive negotiations on nuclear and sanctions issues under the recent ceasefire. And the oil is starting to move again. The Nikkei reports that 98 tankers transited the Strait of Hormuz in a single week — the most since the strait effectively closed back in February. Oil supply has recovered to about 60 percent of pre-conflict levels. Not full normalcy yet — that depends on ships coming back to actually load cargo — but the direction is clear.

Now let's turn to money, because there's a lot moving here. On the front page, the Nikkei reports that Nomura Holdings and the American firm Circle Internet Group plan to launch a new foreign currency settlement service for Japanese companies, starting in 2027. The idea is to use stablecoins — these are digital tokens pegged to a real currency, in this case the US dollar — to make currency conversion instant. Right now, a big yen-to-dollar transaction can take half a day to settle. This would make it immediate. It's Circle's first move into Japan, and it follows a 2025 US law nicknamed the GENIUS law, which has been pushing American stablecoins out into the world.

The markets, meanwhile, had quite a day. The Nikkei average hit a record high — 72,366 yen, up four-point-six percent. What drove it? The American chipmaker Micron Technology gave strong earnings guidance, citing booming demand for AI memory. And when AI memory demand goes up, Japan's whole semiconductor supply chain rides along.

You can see that same AI tailwind in two corporate stories. Kioxia, the Japanese memory chipmaker, announced a stock split — its share price has risen seventy-fold since its IPO, and a split makes the stock more accessible to ordinary investors. And Nintendo is investing 121 billion yen in a brand-new research and development complex in Kyoto, to be completed in 2029, expanding its capacity to build both games and hardware.

But not everything is glowing. The same day the stock market set a record, a 20-year government bond auction came in weak — demand was sluggish. And that connects to a quieter policy shift the Nikkei flags: the government is moving away from balancing its budget year by year, toward managing it over multiple years. In plain terms, that loosens fiscal discipline. The new anchor becomes stabilizing debt relative to the size of the economy. Supporters say it's more realistic. Skeptics — including, it seems, some bond investors — worry it makes Japan's long-term debt path harder to read. Worth watching.

Two more quick notes before we close. Takeda, Japan's biggest pharmaceutical company, has appointed its first female CEO — Julie Kim, a Korean-American executive who came up through the Shire acquisition. And on the imperial succession question, the lower house speaker has approved an outline for reform — allowing female members of the imperial family to keep their status after marriage, and allowing certain men from former imperial branches to return as adopted heirs. Details still to come, but it's a meaningful step on a long-debated issue.

And now, the one that caught my eye — tucked deeper in the paper. China has committed to launching a nationwide long-term care insurance system by the end of 2028. Here's why that matters. China now has 223.6 million people aged 65 and over — that's sixteen percent of the population — and an estimated 35 million who need care. The one-child generation simply cannot shoulder that the old way, through family alone. So even an authoritarian state is being pushed toward something like a welfare program.

But the obstacles are enormous. The income gap between regions is staggering — disposable income in Shanghai is nine-point-two times that of Gansu province. Add chronic shortages of care workers and facilities, and equitable delivery looks very hard. What struck me is the deeper point: demographics force everyone's hand, regardless of ideology. And Japan, the country that aged first, is watching its giant neighbor walk the same road.
