Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Thursday, June 25th, 2026, and there's a single thread running through a lot of today's paper: money is flowing into Japan, and the country is scrambling — in the best sense — to build the plumbing to handle it. Let's get into it.

Our lead story is, on its face, a technical one. The Tokyo Stock Exchange says it will roughly double the capacity of its trading system by November. Right now, the system can process about 830 million orders a day. By November, that figure will jump to around 1.5 billion. This is the biggest upgrade since the exchange's "Arrowhead" trading system launched back in 2010.

Now, why does an obscure piece of market infrastructure lead the Nikkei this morning? Because of what it tells you about the moment Japan is having. Overseas investors have been pouring money into Japanese stocks — there's a phrase being used, "Japan buying," and it's exactly what it sounds like. Daily order volumes hit an average of 230 million in June. That's nearly double a year ago. And earlier this month, the Nikkei index briefly pushed above 70,000 — a remarkable number.

When markets get this busy, systems can buckle. Nikkei points to April of last year, when a geopolitical shock sent traders rushing to sell, and orders spiked to 370 million in a single day. The exchange doesn't want a repeat of that under heavier volumes — a system failure during a panic is exactly when you need the machinery to hold. So they're spending several hundred million yen to make sure it does. The headline isn't really the technology. It's the confidence that all this foreign money is here to stay.

And the government is leaning into that confidence — hard. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi has unveiled a sweeping investment strategy: ¥370 trillion in combined public and private money across seventeen strategic sectors by fiscal 2040. We're talking AI, semiconductors, and something the paper calls "physical AI" — robotics, essentially, machines that think. Of that total, more than ¥230 trillion is expected to come from private companies. The government projects this lifts nominal growth to three-and-a-half percent, with real growth near two percent.

Here's where I'll ask you to keep one eyebrow raised, because Nikkei does too. Over the past fifteen years, despite a parade of similar grand plans, Japan's economy has actually grown an average of just 0.7 percent a year. So there's a real gap between the ambition and the track record. These targets have a way of arriving with great fanfare and quietly missing.

Tied to this, Takaichi also announced a separate special account just for economic security — funding for things like defense and energy, walled off from the general budget. The mechanism uses what are called bridge bonds — short-term government borrowing meant to keep money flowing steadily into critical sectors. The appeal is autonomy: the government can move fast. The concern, which Nikkei flags, is oversight. Money outside the regular budget is money outside the regular scrutiny of parliament.

And the reason economic security is suddenly so urgent? Look at the global AI race. Anthropic's newest model, Claude Mythos 5, just scored 80 percent on a tough software-debugging test — leaving OpenAI's latest at 59 percent and Google's at 55. That's a big gap. And because those same skills are useful for cyberattacks, the US slapped export controls on the technology on June 12th. For Japan, that's a warning shot. If American AI can be cut off, you want your own. Encouragingly, Japan's Sakana AI hit nearly 74 percent using a clever hybrid of open-source models — a homegrown hedge against exactly the kind of US supply disruption we just saw.

Now, all this investment ambition runs into one stubborn reality: interest rates are rising everywhere, and borrowing is getting expensive. The most striking example is in Britain, where ten-year government bond yields have pushed past five percent — higher than during the so-called "Truss Shock" of 2022. And Nikkei identifies a fascinating new culprit: AI companies. Big tech issued $121 billion in corporate bonds last year — four times the recent average — and all that borrowing is competing with governments for the same pool of money. It's a phenomenon they call "crowding out." More demand for credit pushes yields up everywhere, Japan included, where long-term rates have touched 2.8 percent and could breach three.

Let's turn to politics and policy here at home. There's real movement on a food consumption tax cut — and this matters at the kitchen table. A cross-party council has drafted a plan to slash the tax on food to just one percent for two years, starting April 2027. Alongside it: a ¥600 billion annual cash-transfer program, and eventually a permanent income-linked relief system by 2029. Japan's consumption tax touches everyone, so even a temporary cut on food is meaningful — and politically, deeply popular.

Parliament also votes today on a bill targeting election misinformation — making false social media posts that damage election integrity illegal. And after years — decades, really — of dispute, the maglev bullet train is about to get a green light. Shizuoka's governor is set to approve construction of the Chuo Shinkansen at today's assembly session, with environmental agreements expected by mid-July. Shizuoka had long blocked the project over water and environmental concerns, so this is a genuine milestone for a train that can hit speeds rail engineers once only dreamed of.

Abroad, the big development is oil. After a US-brokered ceasefire and temporary sanctions relief, Iran has begun shipping crude again. A tanker left Kharg Island on the 23rd carrying about two million barrels. Nikkei notes the staggering scale of what's at stake — up to $1.3 trillion in potential Iranian oil revenue, if prices hold. But there's a sixty-day negotiating clock running, and if talks collapse, the sanctions snap right back. Relatedly, Oman has opened a toll-free passage through the Strait of Hormuz for some 11,000 seafarers who've been stranded — a quiet humanitarian effort coordinated with the International Maritime Organization.

And keep an eye on the weather, because Asia is. Forecasters warn of a possible "super El Niño," and governments aren't waiting. India has halted sugar exports through September. China is stockpiling coal and added 100 gigawatts of generating capacity. Indonesia has built up eleven months of rice reserves. Vietnam and Malaysia have authorized cloud-seeding. When this many countries fortify their food and energy supplies at once, they're telling you something.

In corporate news, an era ends at Takeda. Christophe Weber, after twelve years, has stepped down. He turned Takeda into a ¥4.5 trillion global pharmaceutical giant, largely through the ¥6 trillion purchase of Shire back in 2019. But Nikkei is clear-eyed about the legacy — questions linger about eroded in-house research and a return on equity stuck below eight percent. Bigger, but not necessarily more inventive.

And there's a quiet space race brewing. China showcased plans for satellite networks of nearly 6,000 — and Chinese firms have applied for over 240,000 orbital slots — all aimed at challenging SpaceX. Here at home, Rakuten plans to launch direct satellite-to-phone service by year's end, partnering with US startup AST SpaceMobile — pointedly not relying on SpaceX, unlike its rivals.

Which brings me to the one that caught my eye. Tucked into the international pages, a sobering note ten years after Britain's Brexit referendum. Among the data points: press-freedom rankings show Hong Kong collapsing from 18th place to 140th. And in Boston — the English town, in Lincolnshire, that voted most heavily to Leave — resentment over migration has only deepened, even as the workforce shifted from European to non-European migrant labor. Politically, the old order is cracking. Nigel Farage's Reform UK now leads the polls, having overtaken the Conservatives entirely. And a newer splinter party just took nearly seven percent in a by-election — three times the Tory vote. A decade on, the question the referendum was meant to settle feels more unsettled than ever.
