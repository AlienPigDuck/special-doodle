Let's begin this morning with a story that gets at something Japan has been wrestling with for a long time — the question of whether all that brilliant research actually goes anywhere.

The Nikkei this morning leads with a striking piece of analysis. Researchers looked at nearly three hundred and fifty thousand patents around the world that came out of partnerships between universities and industry. And when they measured how competitive those patents were — how much real-world value they carried — Japan came in dead last among the G7 nations. The index puts Japan at 0.71. The United States sits at 3.20. Germany at 2.90. So this isn't a narrow gap. It's a chasm.

And here's what makes it sting. Japan performs worst even in the six technology fields that Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's government has singled out as strategic priorities — artificial intelligence, quantum, semiconductors, biotech, fusion energy, and space. These are the areas where Japan says it wants to lead.

So what's going wrong? The Nikkei's answer is blunt. Japanese patents don't translate into business. Universities reward professors for publishing academic papers, not for turning discoveries into products. They lack the intellectual property expertise, and they lack the revenue-sharing arrangements that Western universities use to keep researchers motivated and money flowing.

The single number that tells the whole story: Kyoto University earns about one and a half billion yen a year from licensing its intellectual property. That sounds like a lot until you hear that Stanford and UC Berkeley each earn more than a hundred billion yen. So Japan is producing world-class science and then, essentially, leaving the value on the table.

That theme — good technology, weak commercialization — carries right into our next story, because it's the same anxiety in a different industry.

Japan, the Nikkei reports, has become an autonomous driving backwater. Not a single commercial robotaxi service is operating anywhere in the country. Meanwhile, at least thirty cities around the world — in the US, China, Europe, and Southeast Asia — already have self-driving taxis on the road.

How did Japan fall behind? The turning point came around 2020, what the paper calls an autonomous driving "winter," when investment dried up and Japanese automakers lost their nerve. Then generative AI changed the game. Companies elsewhere figured out how to use AI-generated data to train self-driving systems — and they pulled ahead.

Now Japan is playing catch-up, and it's doing so by leaning on foreign help. Nissan, still recovering from its financial troubles, has partnered with a British startup called Wave Technologies — backed by Nvidia and SoftBank — to begin robotaxi trials in Tokyo in the second half of this year. That's real progress. But the Nikkei raises a worry: if Japan relies on foreign technology for the crucial software, it risks becoming, in the paper's phrase, a mere "assembly shop" — building the cars while someone else owns the brains and, potentially, carries the liability.

There's a more hopeful counterpoint, though, and it involves one of Japan's industrial giants. Nvidia and Mitsubishi Heavy Industries have announced a partnership to build next-generation AI data centers — what the industry now calls "AI factories." These facilities run incredibly hot and consume enormous power. Mitsubishi Heavy will supply the cooling systems and energy management. And here's the interesting angle: Mitsubishi holds a world-class share of the market for gas turbines, the machines that generate electricity. So as AI's appetite for power keeps growing, Mitsubishi is positioning itself not just to cool these data centers but potentially to power them too. That's Japanese industrial strength meeting the AI boom on favorable terms.

Now, let's turn to money and regulation here at home, because two stories caught my attention.

The Financial Services Agency — Japan's financial regulator — has launched an investigation into all roughly one hundred of the country's regional banks. The question: can they survive rising interest rates? After years near zero, higher rates change everything for a bank's balance sheet. The regulator is looking at how these banks manage that risk, and from August it plans detailed hearings with more than ten banks suspected of weak controls — including problems like hidden losses on their bond holdings. Regional banks matter enormously in Japan. They're the lenders to small towns and small businesses, and a wave of instability there would be felt across the country.

And a cautionary tale about who's watching the money. The Economy Minister revealed that a failed payment processor called Zentosin owes some five point three billion yen to merchants — money that was supposed to reach shopkeepers but never did. The government now plans to look at other payment processors, because unlike credit card companies, these firms have had almost no regulatory oversight. As more of our spending goes digital, that's a gap worth closing.

Quickly on politics: the ruling coalition is leaning toward extending the current Diet session by about a week, past the seventeenth, to pass a capital bill — though officially the government insists no extension is needed. And in a notable move on economic security, the Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry approved a subsidy of one hundred and sixty billion yen to Tower Semiconductor, an Israeli company, to make optical communication chips in Toyama and Niigata. That's the largest semiconductor subsidy Japan has ever granted under its economic security law.

Now to the wider world, where the dominant story this morning is oil — and the fear driving it.

Crude prices have spiked to eighty-one dollars a barrel, a one-month high, after both the United States and Iran declared their intent to re-impose a closure of the Strait of Hormuz and resumed military strikes. That June ceasefire everyone hoped for? Gone. And the Strait of Hormuz is the single most important oil chokepoint on earth. For energy importers across Asia — Japan very much included — this is exactly the scenario they dread.

There is one small piece of good news buried in the American economic data. US consumer prices rose three and a half percent year-on-year in June — hot, but cooler than the five percent in May, and below forecasts. That slowdown came largely from earlier drops in gasoline prices. But you can see the problem: with the Middle East flaring up again, those energy gains could reverse fast. Federal Reserve officials are sounding more hawkish, signaling they will not tolerate inflation staying high.

And President Trump has quietly backed away from a controversial plan to charge a twenty percent transit fee on ships passing through Hormuz — a toll that lacked legal footing and would have devastated Asian oil markets. In its place, he's pursuing investment agreements with Gulf states.

I want to pause on Europe, because the numbers there are sobering. A brutal heat wave has claimed an estimated ten thousand lives across the continent since May — roughly five thousand in Germany, twenty-seven hundred in the UK, two thousand in France. Part of the reason the toll is so high: air conditioning is rare. Only about nineteen percent of EU homes have it. And forecasters say a "heat dome" of high pressure may sit over France, Spain, and Germany right through July.

And that brings me to the one that caught my eye — because it's a story about a very Japanese frustration, and it comes from the sea.

International talks in Nagasaki to set Atlantic bluefin tuna quotas collapsed this week without a deal. Japan wanted to raise the catch limit on large fish by twenty-five percent while cutting small fish by six percent. But Mexico made a last-minute demand about Pacific waters, and the whole thing deadlocked.

Here's the human detail that stayed with me. The Nikkei describes Japanese fishing communities living with what they call "a catch that is a curse." When a boat has already hit its quota, the fishermen are forced to throw perfectly good tuna back into the sea — fish that often don't survive. All that effort, all that abundance, and the rules require them to waste it. There's something quietly heartbreaking in that image — bounty turned into loss by the stroke of a regulation nobody could agree to change.
