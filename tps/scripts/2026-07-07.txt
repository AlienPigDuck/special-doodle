Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Tuesday, the seventh of July, 2026, and I'm glad you're with me.

We begin this morning with a story that's easy to overlook, but shouldn't be — because it tells us something about where financial power is heading. The Nikkei reports that over the past decade, China has quietly overtaken the United States as the world's leader in fintech patents. That's financial technology — the software and systems behind digital payments, risk management, and the plumbing of modern finance. Of roughly 120,000 patents filed worldwide, China now accounts for 38 percent. The United States? Just 17 percent.

And it's not only about volume. Nikkei notes that China's patents are increasingly high quality — artificial intelligence for managing financial risk, blockchain applications, the kind of next-generation infrastructure that other countries will one day depend on. State-owned giants like the Industrial and Commercial Bank of China are leading the charge.

Now, why does this matter? Because this isn't happening by accident. It's part of a deliberate Chinese strategy to become a financial superpower — and specifically, to chip away at the dominance of the US dollar. If China builds the pipes through which global money flows, it gains leverage over trade, over payments, and over the international standing of its own currency, the yuan. This is a long game. But it's one Beijing appears to be winning quietly, patent by patent.

And China dominates our front pages this morning in another way — a much sharper one. Nikkei reports that on the sixth of June, China's military test-fired a submarine-launched ballistic missile into the Pacific Ocean, beyond Japan's exclusive economic zone. Interestingly, the People's Liberation Army announced the test in advance — notifying Japan about ninety minutes before launch and identifying the target zone.

The missile was likely the Julang-3, with a range of more than ten thousand kilometers. And that number carries a message. From China's own coastal waters, a missile like that can reach the American mainland. This was a deterrent signal — aimed squarely at Washington, and meant to give the United States pause about coming to Taiwan's defense, particularly as the Trump administration weighs its posture in the region.

That signal doesn't stand alone. On the same day, China and Russia launched joint naval and air exercises off Shandong Province — an operation they're calling "Maritime Union 2026." Those forces then moved into the Pacific for joint patrols. Taken together, this is coordinated pressure on Japan and its allies, and it lands at a moment of real strategic churn.

Which brings us across the Pacific, to a summit that almost didn't have its star guest. NATO is meeting this week — and it's being hosted, notably, by Turkey. Nikkei reports that President Erdoğan personally secured Donald Trump's attendance. Trump, who has hinted at pulling the United States out of NATO altogether, reportedly said he wouldn't show up unless Erdoğan himself was hosting. So Turkey finds itself in an unusual role — the bridge holding together a fraying alliance between America and Europe.

There's movement in the Pacific too. Australia and Fiji have signed a mutual defense treaty — the "Pacific Peace Alliance." For Fiji, it's a first: their very first formal military alliance. For Australia, it's their fourth, after the United States, New Zealand, and Papua New Guinea. And the purpose is clear — to counter China's expanding footprint across the Pacific islands, through both security cooperation and economic investment.

Let me turn now to Japan's own politics and diplomacy. Foreign Minister Mitsutoshi Makuura announced that Japan will formally interpret the Strait of Hormuz as an international waterway under the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea. In plain terms, that guarantees transit rights for all vessels — including military ships. It's a direct response to the recent Iran-US tensions, which rattled that vital shipping lane. A great deal of the world's oil passes through Hormuz, and Japan, which imports nearly all of its energy, has every reason to nail down its legal position.

At home, Prime Minister Takaichi has agreed to appear before the opposition-controlled upper house for an intensive budget committee debate. She's under pressure to move remaining government bills before the July 17th session deadline — including a bill on Imperial succession reform. That's a delicate subject in Japan, touching on the future of the imperial line, and one the opposition wants addressed sooner rather than later.

Now to the theme that runs like a thread through today's paper: artificial intelligence, and how it's reshaping work and business.

Consider Kentaro Kawabe. In June, he stepped down as chairman of LINEYahoo — one of Japan's biggest tech companies. And what did he do next? He left his Tokyo office, bought a campervan, and set off to travel Japan. His new venture is called "KKJJ," and the idea is to build businesses autonomously — run by AI agents. Kawabe says he was inspired by watching AI systems hold a genuine dialogue with each other at a youth entrepreneur summit back in 2025. There's a real philosophical current beneath this. Tokyo University's Yasuo Kuniyoshi argues that humans shouldn't resist AI, but integrate with it. Kawabe's campervan journey is, in a sense, one man trying to figure out what that actually means.

That same AI ambition shows up in more everyday places. The discount grocery chain Trial Holdings is launching a nationwide online supermarket — thirteen thousand products, starting in Kyushu this month, scaling to a hundred locations by 2027. Their edge is data — using AI to optimize stores and build personal shopping assistants. And the autonomous-driving startup Turing has raised 12.6 billion yen from Mitsubishi Corporation and the American chipmaker AMD. Much of that money will go toward buying the powerful chips needed to train its self-driving systems.

Speaking of chips — Nikkei has a term worth knowing: "chipflation." Memory chip prices have surged sixfold over the past year, and that's rippling into the price of smartphones, PCs, and cars. Japanese chipmaker Kioxia has seen its shares climb roughly eightfold this year. Great for chipmakers; harder for the companies buying their products.

A couple of lighter notes before we close. Sapporo Breweries is investing nearly 103 billion yen in a joint venture with Denmark's Carlsberg across Singapore, Vietnam, and Hong Kong — aiming for a tenfold jump in premium beer sales by 2035. And Nikka Whisky is fighting an "outdated" image, opening its first Tokyo flagship bar and launching a youth-focused line called "Nikka Frontier," in a clear-bottle design.

And now, the story that caught my eye — tucked deeper in the paper. Japan's average household financial assets have hit an all-time high: 2.059 million yen, driven by soaring stock holdings and the popularity of NISA tax-free investment accounts, especially among younger workers.

But here's the wrinkle. Beneath that cheerful headline, the picture is splitting in two. Working-age households are pouring money into equities. Retirees, meanwhile, are seeing their savings decline — for the first time in six years. The elderly, on fixed pensions, are drawing down their cash as inflation bites. So even as the stock market lifts younger Japan, older Japan is quietly running down its reserves. A two-tier economy, hidden inside one very good headline.
