It's Tuesday, June 23rd, 2026, and there's a lot moving in the world today — a change of government in Britain, a major rate decision here in Japan, and a Japanese power company making a bold bet on the American heartland. Let's get into it.

We'll start there, with our lead story, because it tells you something about where the global economy is heading. Nikkei reports this morning that JERA — Japan's largest thermal power generator, a joint venture between Tokyo Electric Power and Chubu Electric — is going to build a massive natural gas power plant in the central United States. We're talking about roughly one gigawatt of capacity, an investment of around 500 billion yen, or about 3.3 billion dollars, with a target startup of 2028.

Now, here's what makes this interesting. The plant won't just feed into the grid. It's being built right alongside a major AI data center, and it will supply that facility directly. And the reason for that is simple: America's electricity grid is congested. Building new transmission lines takes years. So if you're a tech company that needs enormous amounts of power right now to run artificial intelligence, you build the power plant on site and skip the queue.

And the demand here is staggering. According to the Nikkei, the major U.S. tech firms have set 2026 investment targets totaling 725 billion dollars — much of it pouring into AI infrastructure. All of that needs electricity. JERA is betting it can take its domestic expertise running gas-fired plants, pair it with cheap, abundant American natural gas, and meet that demand. This is the first time a Japanese power company has done anything like this. Worth watching.

And JERA isn't stopping there — Nikkei notes it's been signing long-term supply contracts and adding more U.S. gas-fired projects as that AI buildout accelerates.

Let's turn to the front page, and to some big political and economic news.

In Britain, Prime Minister Keir Starmer has resigned. After his Labour Party's catastrophic showing in May's local elections, and with pressure building from within his own party, Starmer announced he's stepping down. The tipping point, the Nikkei suggests, was the return of Andy Burnham — the former mayor of Manchester — to Parliament through a by-election. Burnham is now the leading candidate to replace him. Labour will hold a leadership election in July, and the new prime minister is expected to take office by September 1st. We'll come back to what this means for Britain's standing in the world a little later.

Closer to home, two important monetary stories. First, the Bank of Japan has raised its policy interest rate to 1.0 percent. That decision came on June 16th, and it's significant — that's the highest the rate has been in 31 years. After decades of near-zero and even negative rates, Japan is now firmly in a tightening cycle, with the BOJ signaling more increases may come. This reflects persistent inflation, even as the broader economy faces headwinds.

And yet, the yen keeps falling. It slid to 161.80 against the dollar — a two-year low. The reason is that markets expect the U.S. Federal Reserve to keep raising rates, which makes the dollar more attractive. So on June 22nd, Japan's Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama held online talks with U.S. Treasury Secretary Bessent, almost certainly to discuss that yen weakness. The news alone was enough to briefly halt the yen's slide as traders adjusted their positions.

And one more from the front page, a moment of history. Alan Greenspan has died at the age of 100. Greenspan chaired the U.S. Federal Reserve from 1987 to 2006, steering American monetary policy through the 9/11 attacks and the Asian financial crisis. His legacy is genuinely mixed. He was celebrated for guiding years of growth without runaway inflation — but he's also remembered for failing to rein in the housing bubble that burst into the 2008 financial crisis. A towering and complicated figure.

Now, here at home, a few developments worth knowing.

Prime Minister Takita has proposed a striking tax measure: cutting the consumption tax on food from 8 percent down to 1 percent for two years, starting in April 2027. The idea is to ease the cost of living, eventually transitioning to income-linked cash grants instead. But there's a catch — local governments would face a shortfall of 1.6 trillion yen a year, so the government will need to find a way to make them whole.

On the imperial family, the government has unveiled long-awaited revisions to the Imperial Household Law. Under the proposed changes, female members of the imperial family could remain in the household even after they marry — right now, they have to leave. And there'd be a path for male descendants of pre-1947 imperial branches to rejoin. A cabinet decision is targeted for the current Diet session. This goes to the heart of an ongoing question: how to secure the future of an imperial line facing a shrinking number of heirs.

And in Shizuoka, after years of local resistance, the prefectural government is set to approve construction of the Maglev bullet train by July, following 22 community briefings. That clears a major obstacle for one of Japan's signature infrastructure projects.

Let's go international, where there's a real piece of good news. U.S. Vice President Vance has announced that Iran has agreed to international nuclear inspections — a breakthrough after a week of negotiations in Switzerland. Mediated by Pakistan and Qatar, the two sides agreed on a 60-day roadmap toward a final accord covering sanctions relief and safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz. That last point matters enormously for global energy markets, and markets noticed — peace hopes are part of what's lifting stocks right now.

In Colombia, a sharp turn to the right. Far-right candidate Abelardo de la Espriella has won the presidency, defeating the leftist incumbent Gustavo Petro. Nikkei frames this as part of a broader Latin American shift toward Trump-aligned strongmen running on crime and immigration.

And Ukraine has proposed a joint reconstruction fund with Japan — around 240 billion yen in capital, with infrastructure and manufacturing equipment supplied by Hitachi, Mitsubishi Heavy Industries, and Toshiba. The revival is aimed at 2040, against a total reconstruction need estimated at 588 billion dollars.

A few quick business notes. In South Korea, SK Hynix has overtaken Samsung Electronics as the country's most valuable company — the first time in 25 years. The driver? High-bandwidth memory chips, the kind that feed AI servers. Its stock has risen more than fourfold.

There's also a warning sign for Nippon Steel. Its acquisition of U.S. Steel is straining under a debt burden of 2.67 trillion yen, and with American interest rates above 4 percent, its interest expenses have jumped to over 101 billion yen this fiscal year — complicating those big investment promises.

And markets, briefly: the Nikkei 225 hit a fresh all-time high, rising 1.5 percent to 72,353, its eighth straight winning day, buoyed by AI optimism and those Iran peace hopes. But analysts caution the market looks overbought. Real estate stocks, meanwhile, were hammered as bond yields climbed.

And now, the one that caught my eye — tucked deep in the paper, almost a footnote. Japan and France are putting 400 billion yen into a rare-earth recycling plant in southern France, the Calemagis facility, targeting a 2027 startup. Why does this matter? Because rare earths are the hidden ingredients in the permanent magnets that power electric vehicles and defense systems. China controls essentially 100 percent of certain supplies, and it's been tightening exports. The price of dysprosium — one key element — is up 90 percent this year, to 28,000 yen a kilogram. Quietly, this project chips away at that monopoly. It may end up being every bit as strategically important as bringing semiconductor manufacturing home — and almost nobody's talking about it.
