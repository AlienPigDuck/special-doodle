It's Tuesday, June 23rd, 2026, and there is a single thread running through almost everything in the paper today: artificial intelligence, and the staggering amount of electricity, money, and political attention it now demands. Let's get into it.

We start with the lead story, and it's a vivid example of just how physical the AI boom has become. JERA — that's Japan's largest thermal power operator, jointly owned by Tokyo Electric Power and Chubu Electric — is building a brand-new, one-gigawatt gas-fired power plant in the American Midwest. And here's the twist: it's being built right next to a data center, the kind used to train large language models. The investment is 500 billion yen, around 3.2 billion dollars, with operations targeted for 2028.

Now, why co-locate the power plant and the data center? Because the AI industry's appetite for electricity has grown so fast that the existing grid simply can't move power where it's needed quickly enough. So instead of plugging into a congested grid, JERA will supply the data center directly. Nikkei calls this Japan's first major infrastructure bet on AI's explosive electricity demand. And to understand the scale of what they're responding to: major US tech firms are planning a combined 725 billion dollars in investment this year alone. JERA is essentially saying — if the computing power needs electricity, we'll build the electricity right beside it.

That same hunger for power shows up elsewhere this morning. Macquarie, the Australian energy major, is committing 50 billion dollars to battery storage in Japan, where the grid is chronically undersupplied. International money is flooding in, chasing modest but steady returns of four to five percent, all aimed at adding enormous battery capacity by 2030. And on the diplomatic side, Japan's Foreign Ministry is launching what it calls "high-level AI dialogues" with France, India, Brazil, and Malaysia. The buzzword here is "AI sovereignty" — the idea that a country should control its own data and not depend entirely on American or Chinese technology platforms. Japan is trying to build a middle path between the two tech superpowers.

Now to the front page, where there's some genuinely big political news from Britain. Prime Minister Keir Starmer has announced his resignation. After devastating losses in May's local elections, and with roughly 100 Labour MPs turning against him, his position became untenable. The frontrunner to replace him is Andy Burnham — the former mayor of Manchester, who recently returned to Parliament through a by-election. Nikkei reports he already has the backing of around 200 Labour MPs. The leadership contest begins July 9th, and Britain expects a new prime minister by September 1st. For listeners outside the UK, this matters because it signals real instability at the top of a major Western economy and a key American ally.

Closer to home, the Bank of Japan has made a historic move. On June 16th, it raised its policy interest rate to one percent — the highest level in thirty-one years. To put that in perspective, Japan spent decades with rates at essentially zero, even below zero. So one percent is a milestone. But it comes with tension. Government officials worry about rising debt costs and weakening business investment, and that worry runs right through today's paper. Long-term government bond yields have been bouncing between 2.3 and 2.8 percent as markets bet on still more rate hikes under the new Takami administration.

And that constraint is becoming the defining feature of Japanese politics right now. Nikkei reports that Prime Minister Takami's policy options are narrowing precisely because interest rates are climbing. Higher rates mean higher costs to service the national debt, which leaves less room for ambitious spending. You can see Takami trying to thread this needle with a proposal to temporarily cut the consumption tax on food — from eight percent down to one percent, starting in 2027, for two years, then back up to eight. It's a careful, time-limited gesture. But even that would cost local governments an estimated 1.6 trillion yen in lost revenue, which has to be made up somehow.

The currency picture is adding pressure. The yen has fallen to 161.80 against the dollar — its weakest level since July 2024. Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama held emergency talks, by video, with US Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen about the instability in the foreign exchange market. A weak yen makes imports more expensive for ordinary Japanese households, so this isn't an abstract concern.

And one more from the front page, a moment to mark a passing. Alan Greenspan, who chaired the US Federal Reserve from 1987 to 2006, has died at the age of 100. For nearly two decades he was perhaps the most powerful economic figure in the world — they called him "the Maestro." He steered the US through the 1987 Black Monday crash and the 1997 Asian currency crisis. But his legacy is genuinely complicated. Critics argue his light touch on the housing bubble helped set the stage for the 2008 financial crisis. A towering figure, and a contested one.

Turning to the Middle East, where the news is whiplash-inducing. US Vice President JD Vance announced that Iran has agreed to accept international nuclear inspections, with the first expected within the week — the product of intensive negotiations in Switzerland, mediated by Pakistan and Qatar. Both sides agreed to a sixty-day timeline for a comprehensive deal. And yet — within hours of signing, Iran threatened to close the Strait of Hormuz again, citing Israeli military actions in Lebanon. The Strait is the world's most important oil chokepoint, so this matters enormously for global energy prices. Germany's defense minister, meanwhile, is blaming the Trump administration's military strikes back in February for setting off this whole cycle. So: a peace agreement and a blockade threat on the very same day. A reminder of just how fragile all of this remains.

In the corporate world, a remarkable changing of the guard in South Korea. SK Hynix has overtaken Samsung in market value for the first time in twenty-five years. The reason is one word: memory. Specifically, high-bandwidth memory — the specialized chips that Nvidia and other AI companies need. SK Hynix bet hard on that market, and it has paid off, while Samsung's more diversified business lagged behind. It's a clean illustration of how AI is reshuffling the global tech hierarchy.

And here in Japan, after years of deadlock, the maglev train may finally be moving. JR Central is hoping to win approval by July for construction in Shizuoka Prefecture — the one section that's been stalled since 2017 over water and environmental concerns. After twenty-two separate resident briefing sessions, the governor is signaling he's ready to authorize it. A 2036 opening is now possible. The catch: construction costs have doubled, to eleven trillion yen.

A very quick look at markets, and no advice here, just the picture. The Nikkei 225 hit an all-time high of 72,353, climbing eight days straight, driven by AI and semiconductor stocks. But Nikkei sounds a note of caution — valuations are now thirty-five percent above their long-term average, a level of froth the paper compares to conditions before 2008. Worth keeping in the back of your mind.

And now, the one that caught my eye, from deeper in the paper. A Chinese startup called Rokid, based in Hangzhou, is launching AI-enabled eyeglasses in Japan — and it raised more than 600 million dollars through crowdfunding, aiming to sell 700,000 units. What I find fascinating is their strategy. Rather than locking buyers into one proprietary AI, these glasses work with whatever you like — OpenAI, Google, take your pick. In a market full of walled gardens, here's a company betting that openness is the selling point. Whether smart glasses finally catch on this time, who knows. But it's a clever wager on consumer freedom.
