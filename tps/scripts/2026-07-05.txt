Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Sunday, the fifth of July, 2026, and I'm glad you're here.

We begin this morning with the heat. And I don't mean that as a figure of speech. Nikkei's lead story this morning paints a picture of a Japan that is, quite literally, becoming a different country in summer. Tokyo's temperature and humidity now rival the cities of Southeast Asia — think Bangkok, think Jakarta. And the economic cost is starting to come into focus. According to the Nikkei, Japan lost an estimated 2.89 billion hours of labor to extreme heat in 2024. That's double the average for the 2010s. Double, in just a few years.

Now, what's interesting here is how businesses are responding. The construction firm Konoike is doing something genuinely bold — giving workers nine days off per month through September, and shifting work schedules to avoid the peak heat of the afternoon. And climate scientists quoted in the paper make a point that I think is worth sitting with. For years, the strategy has been about cooling people down — air conditioning, cooling vests, that kind of thing. The new thinking is different. It's about reducing the time people spend in the heat at all. That means early-morning work, nighttime shifts, seasonal adjustments, more remote work. It's a fundamental redesign of the working day. And if you think about how much of Japan's economy depends on people working outdoors — construction, agriculture, delivery, logistics — you start to see why this is a front-page story and not a weather report. This is about the shape of the workday itself, changing under our feet.

Let's turn to money and security, because there's an important consumer story on the front page. All fifteen of Japan's major brokerage firms — Nomura, Daiwa, SMBC Nikko among them — have now made "passkey" authentication mandatory for online trading. If that term is new to you, a passkey uses your body to log you in — your fingerprint, your face — instead of a password. This follows a wave of fraudulent stock trading in 2025, where criminals hijacked people's investment accounts. Now the Financial Services Agency and the securities industry require multi-factor authentication both at login and when you withdraw money. A couple of firms, SBI and Rakuten, are keeping limited password exceptions for elderly customers — a small but telling detail about the tension between security and accessibility.

And staying with the theme of preparing for the worst — Japan's trade ministry, METI, is building an artificial intelligence system to coordinate disaster relief. The idea is elegant. During a disaster, the platform would pull together real-time inventory data from both government stockpiles and private retailers, assess the damage, and recommend the best routes to get supplies where they're needed. Development begins in fiscal 2026. And notably, they're designing it to limit data collection — protecting corporate secrets and reducing security risk. In a country that lives with earthquakes and typhoons, this is quietly one of the more consequential projects in the paper.

Now, to politics, where Prime Minister Takaichi is navigating a delicate stretch. Nikkei reports that the stability of her ruling coalition hinges on three thorny questions: a proposed one percent cut to the consumption tax, a reduction in the number of Diet seats, and a plan for a so-called "deputy capital" — a backup seat of government. The Democratic Party for the People is circling as a potential kingmaker, but keeping its distance over policy. And there's a legislative squeeze on. With two weeks left in the session, only 73 percent of government bills have passed — below the usual 78 to 80 percent. Seventeen bills are still pending, including imperial succession reform and rice policy. Expect talk of extending the session.

Which brings us naturally overseas, and to a striking theme running through the international pages this morning: the question of what happens if America steps back. Former Australian Prime Minister Kevin Rudd told Nikkei that middle-power nations — Japan, Australia, South Korea, Canada — should band together to defend the rules-based order. His point is arithmetic: their combined economies are larger than America's. If the US retreats, he argues, these countries could pick up the slack through frameworks like AUKUS and support for struggling UN agencies.

And that anxiety has a hard edge to it in the defense pages. Japan, the UK, and Italy have signed a £4.6 billion contract — that's about 990 billion yen — to develop their next-generation fighter jet, with deployment targeted for 2035. Here's the interesting part: they're actively courting Canada, and eyeing Australia, New Zealand, and Singapore, explicitly as an alternative to relying on American military hardware. Read that again. Close US allies are hedging against American reliability. This week's NATO summit in Ankara is the same story in a different key — European nations scrambling to lock in US commitment with promises of hundreds of billions in defense spending. And a Nikkei commentary gently criticizes Prime Minister Takaichi for skipping that summit, calling it a missed chance to bind Japan closer to Europe just as Europe is looking for partners it can trust.

All of this is set against the backdrop of America's 250th birthday, which the US marked on the Fourth of July. Nikkei's coverage is thoughtful — noting the enduring gap between the Declaration's promise that all are created equal and the reality that Jefferson himself held enslaved people; and today, an America where the top one percent hold thirty percent of the wealth. President Trump, meanwhile, delivered sharply partisan remarks at Mount Rushmore, warning of a "renewed communist threat" ahead of November's midterms. And in Tehran, a state funeral was held for former Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, killed in a US-Israel strike in February — a reminder of how volatile the wider world remains.

Let me bring us back to business, because there are some big numbers here. Micron broke ground on a 1.5 trillion yen factory in Hiroshima to make advanced memory chips for AI servers, with the Japanese government chipping in up to 536 billion yen. Production starts in late 2028. And there's a ripple effect worth understanding: AI servers are gobbling up memory chips so hungrily that prices have surged — one type jumped 40 percent — and that's pushing up the cost of your smartphone. Memory now makes up 34 percent of an iPhone's materials cost, and Apple is reportedly considering price increases. So the AI boom you keep hearing about? It may show up in your next phone bill.

Two more quick ones. Takeda Pharmaceutical has appointed Julie Kim, a Korean-American executive, as its first female CEO. And Sony has announced it will stop selling physical PlayStation discs from January 2028, going fully digital. Convenient, yes — but it means the end of the used-game market, and another blow to the game shops on the high street.

And that brings me to the one that caught my eye this morning, buried deep in the paper, and it's about sheep. In Australia, the beloved shepherd dog — that icon of the outback — is being quietly replaced by drones and artificial intelligence. Facing chronic labor shortages, farms are turning to technology. A startup called SkyKelpie already has drones managing more than 600,000 animals. And a New Zealand company, Halter, makes solar-powered smart collars that let farmers herd and monitor cattle remotely, checking their health from a screen. I find this oddly moving — the collie giving way to the quadcopter. It's the same story we opened with, really. Everywhere, from a Tokyo construction site to an Australian sheep station, human beings are redesigning how work gets done, whether because of the heat, or the labor, or the sheer relentless pace of the machines. The world is changing shape, and the papers, this morning, are full of it.
