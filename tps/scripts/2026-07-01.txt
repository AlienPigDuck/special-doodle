Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Tuesday, the first of July, 2026, and there's a lot moving today — currencies, semiconductors, an old name back in power in Peru, and a rather alarming story buried deep in the paper about counterfeit USB drives inside Japan's military. Let's get into it.

We start with the yen, because everything else this morning seems to orbit around it. On June 30th, the Japanese yen fell to 162 against the U.S. dollar. To put that in perspective — that is the weakest the yen has been since December of 1986. Nearly forty years. Think about that for a moment. The yen has not been this cheap against the dollar since before some of you listening were born.

So why now? A few things are pushing in the same direction. American economic data has come in solid, which strengthens the dollar. There's geopolitical anxiety out of the Middle East, and in uncertain times, money flows toward the dollar as a safe haven. And crucially, markets have stopped expecting the Bank of Japan to step in and prop up the yen. That expectation of intervention used to act as a kind of floor. Now that floor has been pulled away, and traders are openly talking about the yen sliding further — toward 165 per dollar.

The Nikkei describes this as a "one-strong-dollar" environment, and it cuts both ways for Japan. A weak yen is brutal for importers — anything Japan buys from abroad, from fuel to food, costs more. And we'll see that pain ripple through several stories today. But it's a gift for inbound tourism. Foreign visitors find Japan astonishingly cheap right now, and that's one of the few bright spots.

Here's where it gets interesting. The yen's weakness is partly a story about the dollar's dominance — and Japan is quietly looking for ways around that dominance. According to the Nikkei this morning, Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi is visiting India from today through July 3rd, and during her talks with Prime Minister Modi, she'll announce that Japan and India will explore direct currency trading between the yen and the Indian rupee. Direct trading means bypassing the U.S. dollar entirely — Japanese and Indian businesses could transact without converting through dollars first, cutting costs. It's a small technical step with a larger meaning: two major Asian economies deepening their ties and, ever so gently, reducing their dependence on the American currency.

And it's not the only thread connecting Tokyo and New Delhi. Japan's National Institute of Informatics and India's IIT are partnering to build multilingual AI language models together, with a goal of bringing 500 Indian researchers to Japan by 2030. So this isn't a one-off visit — it's a relationship being built across currency, technology, and talent.

Now, that Middle East tension I mentioned — it's showing up directly in your wallet if you're flying anywhere this summer. Both ANA and JAL have raised their fuel surcharges on international flights for July and August to record highs. Flights to Europe now carry a surcharge of 65,000 yen — that's triple what it was. The cause is jet fuel prices, with Singapore kerosene pushing past 178 yen per barrel as Middle East supply worries bite. Some relief is expected by autumn, but for now, summer travel just got more expensive.

That same supply disruption is hammering Japanese industry. Naphtha sales — that's crude gasoline, the raw material for plastics and chemicals — fell nearly nineteen percent in May. Crude oil imports briefly dropped to levels not seen since 1989 before Japan steadied itself by buying more from the U.S. and other non-OPEC sources. And the root of the standoff: the Nikkei reports U.S.–Iran talks remain deadlocked over the Strait of Hormuz, where Iran is insisting on charging "service fee" tolls on shipping passing through. So far, no direct negotiations are planned. That strait carries a huge share of the world's oil, and as long as it's contested, these ripples keep reaching Japan.

Let's turn to the home front, where the government is making a real philosophical shift. On June 30th, it approved a revised economic policy framework — the document the Japanese call the Bonea — that moves away from austerity and toward growth. The idea of holding down every line of the budget is out. In its place: "selective spending increases," a willingness to run temporary deficits if they're justified by growth investments, and a new investment fund with no upper spending limit. The target is one percent real GDP growth. This is a meaningful tonal change for a country long anxious about its enormous public debt.

There are signs the economy needs that push. The job market for new high school graduates has now slipped below break-even for eight straight months — the ratio of job offers to applicants sat at 0.99 in May. When that number falls below one, it means there are fewer jobs than job-seekers. The Nikkei links it to business caution and to companies investing in automation rather than hiring. Some better news: corporate pension funds posted their best returns in a decade, 1.71 percent in fiscal 2025, helped by rising interest rates lifting bond yields. Though, worth noting, that still trails inflation.

A significant constitutional moment, too: the government approved a revised Imperial Household Law. It will now allow female imperial family members to remain in the family after they marry, and it permits adopting male descendants from former imperial branches into the line of succession. But — and this is the heart of it — the reform prioritizes preserving the male line over any broader fix to Japan's succession crisis. The imperial family is shrinking, and this is a cautious, conservative answer to that problem.

Quickly around the world: in Peru, Keiko Fujimori — daughter of the late strongman Alberto Fujimori — won a razor-thin presidential runoff with 50.1 percent. She invoked her father's tough-on-security legacy while keeping her distance from his authoritarian record, and she inherits a fractured congress and rising drug violence. In South Korea, Han Sung-suk becomes only the second woman ever to serve as prime minister. And in Europe, the EU's tariff on steel imports above quota jumps to 50 percent today, double the old rate, aimed at Chinese oversupply — though Japan looks likely to keep preferential treatment under its economic partnership agreement.

In business, a theme worth watching: the global scramble for chips. Samsung and SK Hynix have jointly pledged 80 trillion won to build four new chip plants in Gwangju by 2030, chasing AI demand. Meanwhile Chinese makers are racing to close a two-to-three-year gap with Japanese rivals on critical chip materials. And here's a knock-on effect — Marubeni is taking full control of refurbished phone seller IOSYS, betting that AI-driven memory demand will keep pushing new phone prices up, making used devices more attractive. Marubeni wants to sell over 200 million used devices a year by 2030.

And now, the one that caught my eye — and frankly, it's unsettling. The Nikkei reports that a single Self-Defense Force soldier, by chance, discovered malware-infected counterfeit Chinese USB drives in use inside a highly classified military network. Read that again: fake USB sticks, carrying viruses, plugged into Japan's most sensitive systems. And it gets worse. The infected drives slipped past initial inspection. The security software meant to catch them was misconfigured. And procurement records didn't even match between the SDF and Ishikawa Prefecture — nobody could fully account for where these drives came from.

Even after realizing that counterfeit Chinese drives were circulating widely in the commercial market, the military chose to stay quiet rather than sound the alarm. This is a story about systemic control failures, caught not by a sophisticated audit but by one alert soldier noticing something was wrong. In an era of cyber warfare, the most basic supply chain — a USB stick — turned out to be the weak link.
