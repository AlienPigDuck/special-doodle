Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Saturday, the fourth of July, 2026. I'm glad you're here.

We begin this morning with trains — because in Japan, so much really does begin with trains. Keisei Electric Railway announced plans for a new paid express service that will link Japan's two great gateways: Narita Airport, out east of Tokyo, and Haneda, much closer to the city center. Right now, getting between the two takes about a hundred minutes. Keisei wants to cut that dramatically, and it hopes to launch sometime in the 2030s. The Ministry of Land, Infrastructure, Transport and Tourism has signed off.

Now, why does connecting two airports make the front page? Because this is really a story about overtourism, and about geography. Japan welcomed 24.1 million international visitors in the last fiscal year — a staggering number — and most of them pile into the same handful of places. Kyoto, Tokyo, Osaka. The idea here is that if you can move arriving tourists smoothly from Narita over to Haneda, you can then feed them onto Haneda's dense network of domestic flights and send them out to the regions — to places that would love the visitors and the spending, and that have room to breathe. It's an elegant bit of infrastructure logic: use one train to spread the wealth across an entire country. Whether tourists actually take the detour, of course, is another question. But the ambition is clear.

Let's stay with the theme of Japan recalibrating its place in the world — because that runs through a lot of this morning's paper.

Nikkei reports that, for the first time in thirty-one years, China now supplies less than half of Japan's imported textiles. The figure slipped to 49.8 percent in 2025. For three decades, "made in China" was simply the default for the clothes Japanese consumers wore. That's ending. Chinese labor costs have climbed, and Japanese apparel makers are moving production to Vietnam, Bangladesh, and Indonesia.

But this isn't only about wages. It's also about politics. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's comments back in November of 2024 about a possible Taiwan contingency rattled the relationship with Beijing, and there are reports of stricter Chinese customs inspections hitting Japanese traders. So companies are hedging — spreading their supply chains away from a single dependency. It's a quiet, structural shift, and thirty-one years is a long time to reverse a habit.

That same tension shapes Takaichi's diplomacy abroad. She's just wrapped up her first visit to India as prime minister, and she came home with a commitment of two trillion yen in Japanese private investment, along with deeper defense cooperation. Read that alongside another story from today: South Korea is restarting tungsten mining for the first time in thirty-two years, positioning itself to supply Japan and reduce everyone's reliance on China for a mineral that's essential to semiconductors. There's a pattern here. Japan, India, South Korea — democracies in the region quietly building alternatives to Chinese influence and Chinese supply. India especially matters as a counterweight to China's Belt and Road push across Asia.

Now let me turn to something that deserves your attention, even though it lives in the numbers — Japan's government bond market.

The yield on new ten-year government bonds briefly touched 2.81 percent on Thursday. That's the highest level since October of 1996 — a thirty-year high. To understand why that's remarkable, remember that for most of the last decade, Japanese interest rates hovered near zero. The whole country ran on cheap money. So a move to 2.81 percent is a genuine change of era.

What's driving it? Fiscal worry. The government's latest economic policy guideline, issued on the thirtieth of June, quietly dropped the phrase "fiscal consolidation" — the language about getting the national debt under control. And it leaned, implicitly, on the Bank of Japan to go easy on raising rates. Investors read that as a signal that Japan is choosing to spend and to tolerate more inflation, which is exactly the sort of thing that makes bondholders nervous. So they sold, and yields rose. It's the market's way of asking a hard question about how Japan pays its bills over the long run.

There's a related note on the Financial Services Agency, which is tightening the rules around large shareholders. If you hold five percent or more of a Japanese company, you already have to disclose it. Now the FSA wants far more detail — what proposals you intend to make, and when — and it's planning to raise penalties for false or missing filings to roughly seventy times current levels by 2027. Why now? Activist investors have gotten remarkably busy. Nikkei reports 139 shareholder proposals in the single month of June. That's a record, and regulators want transparency to keep pace.

On the household side, the news is more sober. This spring's wage negotiations delivered raises averaging 4.69 percent at small firms — and for the second year running, that fell short of the 6 percent target. Small companies simply haven't been able to pass their higher costs along to customers, and their profitability lags. So while headline wage growth sounds healthy, the middle-class squeeze continues. That's the everyday reality behind those rising interest rates.

A few brighter numbers before we go. Japan's public pension fund, the GPIF, reported an operating profit of 41.4 trillion yen for the last fiscal year — its second-highest on record — riding this AI-driven surge in global stock markets. And Japanese fruit is having a moment abroad: fruit exports are up 20 percent over five years, reaching 1.363 trillion yen, led by those luxurious Shine Muscat grapes that Asian buyers can't get enough of. Though farmers warn of two threats — a shortage of agricultural labor, and seed piracy, where prized varieties get quietly copied overseas.

Not everything is rising, mind you. JR Hokkaido's eight money-losing rail lines saw their annual operating losses widen to 15.5 billion yen. A fare hike of 7.6 percent last April simply couldn't offset falling ridership and the cost of recovering from disasters. And Japan Post is raising Yupack parcel rates by an average of 10 percent from October, blaming labor and fuel costs. The cost of moving things — people and packages — keeps climbing.

And that brings me to the story that caught my eye this morning, buried well inside the paper, and I think unfairly so.

Kyoto University has just been named Japan's third "Leading Research University." With that title comes 20 billion yen this fiscal year — but the money isn't the interesting part. The reform is. Kyoto plans to dismantle its old academic structure entirely. For generations, Japanese universities have been organized into what are called "lecture halls" — rigid units, each essentially the fiefdom of a single senior professor. Kyoto has about a thousand of them. By 2029, it intends to collapse those thousand silos into roughly twenty broad, cross-disciplinary departments.

Why does this matter beyond Kyoto? Because it's really Japan confronting its own demographics. The country's faculty is aging, and its brightest young researchers keep leaving for universities in the United States and Europe, where they can lead their own work sooner. Kyoto's goal is to double the number of young research leaders to 550 by 2050. Behind the dry administrative language is a genuine reckoning — an acknowledgment that Japan's system of deference and hierarchy, however venerable, has been holding back its best young minds in fundamental science. Reorganizing a university chart may sound modest. But over the next decade, this is exactly the kind of change that determines whether a country keeps inventing things. I'll be watching it.
