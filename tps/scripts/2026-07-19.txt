It's Sunday, the nineteenth of July, 2026, and there's a thread running through the paper today that ties together the front page, the markets, and even a story buried deep inside. That thread is artificial intelligence — and specifically, the growing gap between how fast it moves and how slowly the rest of us can keep up.

Let's start with the lead, because it's genuinely unsettling. Nikkei reports this morning on new research from the University of Toronto and the American security firm CloudStrike showing that AI-powered cyberattacks have tripled in speed since 2021. Autonomous AI agents — programs that operate on their own, without a human at the keyboard — are now finding software weaknesses and breaking into corporate networks three times faster than they did five years ago.

Let me give you the number that stopped me. There's a company called Qualys that tracks these things. In 2021, the average time between a software vulnerability being disclosed and an actual attack exploiting it was ninety-eight minutes. By last year, 2025, that window had shrunk to twenty-nine minutes. And in the fastest cases — twenty-seven seconds. In eighty-eight percent of cases, the attack begins before the company can even install the patch that would have protected it.

Here's why this matters, and it's really a story about asymmetry. AI can help defenders too — it's been shown to cut the time it takes to contain a breach by twenty-eight percent. But defenders are slowed down by exactly the things that make them responsible. They have to worry about liability. They need a human being to approve major actions. They can't just let an AI loose on their own network and hope for the best. Attackers face none of that. A criminal deploying an AI has no compliance department, no legal review, no accountability. So the machines built to break things are being unleashed, while the machines built to protect us are kept on a leash. That's the imbalance the researchers are warning about.

Now, that theme — the machines racing ahead of human oversight — carries right through to today's markets, and they had a rough day. The Nikkei 225 fell four percent on Thursday's trading, closing at just over sixty-four thousand points. The real damage was in semiconductors. The Nikkei Semiconductor Index has now dropped thirty percent from its recent highs. Individual names got hammered — the memory chip maker Kioxia fell somewhere between forty and fifty percent.

So what happened? In short, the market got nervous that the AI boom might be running on hope. There's been enormous investment in chips and data centers on the assumption that demand for AI would keep climbing forever. But two things spooked investors. One, Meta announced cloud service plans that raised questions about how sustainable all that spending really is. And two, there's growing awareness that China is catching up in advanced memory chips faster than expected. When a market has been priced for perfection, it doesn't take much to shake it.

And here's the tell for what comes next. Rita Saito, chief equity strategist at JPMorgan Securities, told Nikkei she sees near-term support around sixty-three thousand — but says a real recovery depends on what the big American cloud companies say in their late-July earnings. If they confirm they're still spending heavily on AI, confidence returns. If they hesitate, this could get worse. The whole global market, in other words, is holding its breath waiting to hear whether the AI investment story is real.

It wasn't just Tokyo. Over in Seoul, the situation has been genuinely alarming. South Korea's KOSPI index has triggered thirty-seven "circuit breakers" this year — those are automatic trading halts that kick in when prices move too violently. That's more halts already than in all of 2008, the year of the global financial crisis. A big part of the problem has been leveraged products — investments that amplify both gains and losses. Korean authorities have now moved to restrict single-stock leveraged funds, effective immediately.

And feeding all of this: China's Moonshot AI unveiled a new model on Thursday called Kimi K3. Independent evaluations suggest it approaches — or matches — the capability of Anthropic's latest model. That closed the perceived gap with American AI faster than experts thought possible, and it knocked American tech stocks. Alphabet and Meta both fell two to three percent. So you can see how it all connects: the fear that China is catching up is rippling through markets on both sides of the Pacific.

Let me turn now to a very different kind of failure — a human one — on the domestic front page. Japan's Financial Services Agency will begin inspecting regional banks and credit unions this summer, after the bankruptcy of a payment processor called Zenshin. And the numbers here are staggering. Zenshin had been falsifying its financial records for at least twenty years. Twenty years. And yet sixty financial institutions kept lending to it — leaving them holding more than a hundred and ten billion yen in loans with no collateral behind them.

Why does this happen? Because many of these smaller lenders simply didn't understand the business they were lending to. Payment processing has its own peculiar economics, and banks unfamiliar with it took Zenshin's inflated figures at face value. It's a very old-fashioned scandal, really — a story about due diligence that wasn't done. And it's a reminder that even in an age of AI-driven everything, sometimes the failure is just people not checking the books.

Two quicker items from the front page. Mori Building, the developer behind Tokyo's Toranomon Hills, is planning another major expansion right next door — a forty-thousand-square-meter complex with offices, homes, a hotel, and cultural space, extending a business district that already employs around thirty thousand people. Completion is expected around 2031.

And the Linear Chuo Shinkansen — Japan's next-generation magnetic-levitation train between Tokyo and Nagoya — has finally cleared its last hurdle. Shizuoka Prefecture and JR East signed an environmental agreement on Friday, ending a long standoff over the one unbuilt section of the route. Groundbreaking is targeted for year-end. But don't hold your breath — the tunneling involves fault zones and depths of fourteen hundred meters, and completion now looks unlikely before 2036. That's a full decade later than originally planned.

A word on politics and security. China test-launched an advanced submarine-launched ballistic missile last month, the JL-3, with a range of over ten thousand kilometers. That's sharpened a debate inside Prime Minister Takaichi's administration about Japan's long-held non-nuclear principles. Defense Minister Shinjiro Koizumi said this week that Japan can no longer treat nuclear issues as taboo — that leaving them out of the conversation entirely may itself weaken national security. This is delicate territory in the only country ever attacked with nuclear weapons, and it's worth watching how far this discussion goes.

And which brings me, finally, to the one that caught my eye — buried deep in the paper, but I think one of the most telling stories of the day. As of the fifteenth of July, China has banned AI "romantic partners" for minors. A new regulation prohibits emotional-AI services — the kind that mimic human conversation and affection — from offering virtual boyfriends, girlfriends, or family members to anyone underage. It also requires the apps to warn users after two hours of continuous use.

What's driving this? The data is striking. Eighty-four percent of users of these services report forming an emotional attachment. Forty-two percent use them every single day. Beijing has decided that this kind of dependency is a threat to social cohesion — that people, especially young people, forming their closest bonds with software is something the state needs to intervene in.

And here's what makes it genuinely significant. China is out ahead of the West on this. The European Union's AI Act only addresses manipulating vulnerable people for commercial gain. Japan hasn't drafted anything comparable at all. So as these AI companions spread worldwide — through ChatGPT-style chatbots, through AI streamers — the first country to draw a hard line around our emotional lives is China. Whatever you make of the messenger, the question is one every society will eventually have to answer.
