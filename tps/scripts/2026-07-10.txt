Good morning, and welcome to The Papers Say — Japan's Nikkei newspaper, in English, every morning. It's Friday, the tenth of July, 2026, and there's a lot moving in the world this morning, so let's get right to it.

Our lead story takes us to the Middle East, and then, remarkably, straight back home to the bond market in Tokyo. According to the Nikkei this morning, the confrontation between the United States and Iran has escalated sharply. On the ninth of July, US Central Command announced it had struck 170 targets inside Iran — air defense systems, surveillance facilities along the coast — in retaliation for Iranian attacks on commercial ships in the Strait of Hormuz. Iran's Revolutionary Guards say they hit back at American bases in Bahrain, Jordan, and Kuwait. And then came the line that really rattled markets: Iran's parliamentary speaker, Ghalibaf, declared that the Strait of Hormuz is under Iran's control alone — a not-so-subtle hint that Tehran could try to close it.

Now, why does a strait most of us couldn't find on a map matter so much? Because a huge share of the world's oil passes through it. Close it, or even threaten to, and oil prices jump, and inflation fears come roaring back.

And here's where it lands on Japan's doorstep. The Nikkei reports that Japan's ten-year government bond yield hit 2.9 percent yesterday — the highest in thirty years. That's a very big deal. For most of the last three decades, Japan lived in a world of near-zero interest rates. Now the ten-year is closing in on three percent, a level that would have seemed almost unimaginable not long ago. Higher yields mean the government pays more to borrow, and Japan carries an enormous debt load. So this is a genuine headache for Prime Minister Takaichi's administration, which is trying to keep the economy growing without spooking the very markets that finance it.

And that tension is playing out right now in Tokyo. Chief Cabinet Secretary Nori Kihara said the government is keeping, in his words, "high vigilance" on interest rates, and promised fiscal policy won't "carelessly pursue expansion and lose market confidence." At the same time, the government is revising its economic framework to explicitly respect the Bank of Japan's independence — citing Article 3 of the Bank of Japan Law. The subtext there is important. Markets have suspected the government of quietly resisting rate hikes. This is Tokyo saying: we're not leaning on the central bank. Whether investors believe that is another question entirely.

Let's stay with policy for a moment, because the Diet — Japan's parliament — is racing against a clock. Today, the upper house is expected to pass three government bills, on personal data protection, digital government, and labor insurance. But thirteen more bills are still waiting, and the session ends on the seventeenth. That's roughly a fifth of the agenda in limbo, with the opposition slowing things down by demanding budget deliberations and the prime minister's presence.

One bill worth watching is imperial succession reform. It would allow male offspring of male-line imperial descendants to inherit the throne — an attempt to shore up a shrinking imperial family. The ruling coalition expects it to pass this session, though there's friction from conservatives who want constitutional safeguards first.

And a quick note from the political margins: Yamamoto Taro, leader of the left-wing party Reiwa Shinsengumi, has resigned, citing speeding violations and health concerns. The party will pick a successor later this month, and then — interestingly — change its name.

Now, to a story that's a little unsettling if you've ever bought anything online. The Nikkei's front page reports that counterfeit USB memory drives are flooding Amazon and other retailers — devices that lie about how much they can actually store. When Nikkei analyzed 200 Amazon-recommended products, about 20 percent had reports of capacity fraud. All five counterfeit samples they bought and tested had falsified storage. And here's the danger: write data beyond the real capacity, and it's simply lost — gone, unrecoverable. What triggered this investigation was genuinely alarming — infected counterfeit USB drives turned up in use by Japan's Ground Self-Defense Force. Nikkei says Amazon's screening of sellers is inadequate, because on those shared product pages, the cheapest listing floats to the top. Amazon acknowledged the problem but has done little about it.

Let's turn to artificial intelligence, which is threading through nearly everything this morning. The government wants to bring AI into 119 emergency calls — that's Japan's equivalent of 911 — by 2030. The system would analyze a caller's voice in real time, judge how urgent the situation is, and recommend a response to dispatchers. This is really about staffing shortages, especially in rural fire and ambulance services. Humans stay in charge — the AI advises.

That fits a broader push. The Takaichi government has identified twenty priority sectors for what it calls "vertical AI" — AI built for one specific domain, like shipbuilding or drug discovery. And there's a striking example already: Taisei Corporation and the robotics firm Fanuc have built an AI-powered automated warehouse that doubles storage capacity and moves 450 to 500 cases an hour — about triple a human's pace — handling mixed loads that machines usually can't manage.

Overseas, the AI story keeps writing itself. Taiwan's exports in the first half of the year jumped 47 percent to a record 416.6 billion dollars — and nearly 79 percent of that was IT equipment and semiconductors, driven by demand for AI servers. India and Australia have finally signed a uranium supply deal after more than a decade of talks. India wants to expand nuclear capacity from under nine gigawatts today to a hundred gigawatts by 2047 — partly to power the data centers that AI requires.

And China's automakers are on a tear abroad. Domestic vehicle sales fell 21 percent in the first half after EV subsidies were cut back — but exports surged 65 percent, past five million vehicles, as companies like BYD and Chery chase overseas markets.

From the NATO summit, the alliance reaffirmed its collective defense commitment — Article 5, the principle that an attack on one is an attack on all. But President Trump criticized allied spending and signaled that American backing can't be taken for granted. European nations pledged 50 billion dollars in defense investment to keep the US at the table. The Nikkei frames this as a slow drift toward what it calls "NATO 3.0" — a Europe leaning less on Washington.

Two quick business notes at home. Japan's telecom giants are becoming banks. NTT Docomo launched a financial holding company on the first of July — its banking arm is now "Docomo Bank" — bundling banking, securities, and insurance. KDDI has done much the same with auFinancial Services. Why? Cellular growth has flattened to single digits, so they're chasing your wallet through points, apps, and loyalty. And Seven & i Holdings reported a 24 percent jump in quarterly profit, helped by gasoline sales overseas and a recovery in its domestic convenience stores.

And now, the one that caught my eye. Deep in the international pages, Huawei — China's telecom giant, boxed in by US export controls — has proposed what it grandly calls "Tau Scaling." For decades, chipmakers followed Moore's Law: cram ever-smaller circuits onto a chip. But the machines that do that miniaturization are exactly what the US won't sell to China. So Huawei's answer is to stop shrinking and start stacking — piling chips on top of each other to shorten the distance signals travel, and speed things up that way. It's a clever framing of a constraint as a philosophy. But Western analysts, Nikkei notes, are skeptical — Huawei is still far behind TSMC and Nvidia, and this looks more like propaganda than breakthrough. Still, there's something telling about it: when you can't win the race everyone else is running, you rename the race. Necessity, quite literally, dressed up as invention.
