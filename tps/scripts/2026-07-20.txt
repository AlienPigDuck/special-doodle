We begin this morning in the Nordic countries, where a decision decades in the making has finally arrived. Finland's parliament has approved constitutional amendments that will allow nuclear weapons to pass through its territory in peacetime, and to be deployed there in a crisis. For a country that has spent forty years keeping nuclear arms firmly at arm's length, this is a genuine turning point.

To understand why, you have to look east, across the longest border any country shares with Russia. Finland watched Russia invade Ukraine, and it drew a hard conclusion. According to the Nikkei this morning, intelligence assessments show Russia has increased its artillery production by nearly sixty percent, and one report describes Moscow as, quote, "factually preparing for the next war." Finland joined NATO after the invasion. Now it's going a step further, into the realm of nuclear deterrence itself.

But there's a cost, and it's already arriving. Russia has closed five border checkpoints with Finland. It's raised rail transport costs eightfold — an increase that threatens fertilizer imports and supply chains reaching all the way into Central Asia. And here's the deeper worry that experts raise in the Nikkei: leaning on nuclear deterrence carries its own risks. Deterrence can steady a situation, or it can escalate one. Finland is betting it steadies. Russia's response suggests the bet won't be cheap.

Let's come home to Japan now, and to a story that speaks quietly but touches an enormous number of lives. Japan's property management companies are walking away from small apartment buildings. If your condominium has fewer than forty units, the Nikkei reports, your management company may simply decide it's no longer worth the trouble. Costs are up, workers are scarce, and the math no longer works. Some firms are demanding annual fee increases of six hundred thousand yen — that's roughly four thousand dollars — just to stay on.

The numbers tell the story. Nearly forty-eight percent of residents have seen their management fees rise in the past three years. A quarter of all buildings have switched management companies in the past decade — and for buildings built before 1974, that figure climbs to half. Now, why does this matter beyond the inconvenience? Because without professional management, aging residents are left to maintain aging buildings on their own. Properties fall into disrepair, values drop quickly, and whole neighborhoods can slide with them. This is what Japan's shrinking, aging population looks like at street level — not a dramatic headline, but a slow erosion of the ordinary places people live.

Now, two stories this morning show artificial intelligence reshaping who wins and who loses at work, and I want to put them side by side.

The first is stark. Among Japan's IT engineers, the Nikkei reports a dramatic split. Freelancers doing upstream work — project management, the higher-level thinking — have seen their wages rise twenty percent since 2022. But engineers doing downstream tasks, like HTML coding, have watched their pay fall by half. The reason is generative AI, which can now write code that people used to be paid for. And here's the detail that stopped me: some firms have recorded seven-hundred-fold differences in productivity among engineers using the exact same AI tools. Seven hundred to one. What matters now, it seems, isn't baseline technical skill. It's whether you've mastered the machine.

That same logic is showing up in the boardroom. Mercari, the online marketplace company, has just combined two senior roles — chief technology officer and chief human resources officer — under a single executive, Shunya Kimura. Think about what that says. Technology and people, once separate departments, are now one job. The idea is that adopting AI isn't a technical project you hand to the engineers. It's about how humans and machines will work side by side, every day. Mercari is treating that as a single question, and other firms are watching.

Staying with money and how it moves, there's a fascinating first this morning. The logistics company AZ-COM Maruwasaki Holdings will begin paying around 2,300 of its independent truck drivers and logistics partners using JPYC — a digital currency pegged to the yen, what's called a stablecoin. This is Japan's first large-scale corporate use of one. The appeal is practical: no transaction fees, and drivers can convert to cash immediately. For small operators living with tight cash flow in a labor-starved industry, that speed matters. The company is putting a billion yen behind the partnership. It's a small step that could point toward something much larger in how Japanese businesses pay each other.

To politics now, where Prime Minister Takaichi is making a bold and risky move. Her government wants to cut the consumption tax on food — from eight percent down to just one percent, for two years, beginning in April 2027. For households squeezed by rising prices, that's real relief at the grocery store. But it comes at a price of roughly five trillion yen in lost revenue. And this lands at an awkward moment. Both the IMF and the OECD have warned about Japan's fiscal sustainability, and the markets are listening. The Nikkei reports that the yield on ten-year Japanese government bonds has climbed sharply — from 1.66 percent last October to 2.9 percent on the ninth of July. That's investors asking a pointed question: can Japan keep spending like this? Prime Minister Takaichi is offering help at the checkout while the bond market taps its foot. How she squares those two things will define her government.

A few quick looks around the world. In New York, Mayor Eric Adams says he's weighing whether to arrest Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu when he arrives for the UN General Assembly in September, citing an International Criminal Court warrant. Legally, it's largely symbolic — the United States isn't an ICC member and has no obligation to comply — but as a political statement, it's remarkable.

In Taiwan, the ruling Democratic Progressive Party is fielding nine women among nineteen announced mayoral candidates for local elections on the twenty-eighth of November — a deliberate strategy to win back the urban votes it lost in 2022.

And across Southeast Asia, the Nikkei describes ASEAN nations carefully keeping their balance between Washington and Beijing. Trust in the United States still edges out China — forty-four percent to just under forty percent — but deep unease about American unpredictability is pushing these countries to hedge, deepening ties with Japan, Australia, India, and Europe as insurance.

Before we go, here's the one that caught my eye — tucked into the business pages. San Marc Holdings, a restaurant group, has just moved its headquarters from Okayama to Kyoto. Why? To borrow Kyoto's global reputation as a food city — and to use it as a springboard overseas. The company's ambition is delightfully specific: four hundred international franchises by the 2030s, built around, of all things, beef cutlets. Gyutan, the grilled beef tongue that's a specialty of northern Japan, headed for Southeast Asia and North America. There's something wonderful about a company betting that the world is ready for Japanese beef tongue, and moving to Kyoto to make it happen. Japanese food has conquered global cities one dish at a time — sushi, then ramen, then karaage. Perhaps gyutan is next.
