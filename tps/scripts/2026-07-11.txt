We begin this morning where the whole world seems to be looking — the Persian Gulf, and the growing confrontation between the United States and Iran.

According to the Nikkei this morning, the military standoff has escalated sharply. On the seventh of July, Iran attacked three commercial ships. Then it fired ten ballistic missiles at a U.S. military base in Jordan, and the United States retaliated. And now the fear that hangs over all of this is the Strait of Hormuz — that narrow neck of water at the mouth of the Persian Gulf, through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil passes every single day. When Hormuz gets tense, the whole global economy holds its breath.

And here's why this matters so much for Japan. This is a country that imports nearly all of its energy. When oil supply gets shaky, Japan feels it immediately — in factories, in shipping, in the price of nearly everything.

Now, layer on top of that a moment of real confusion in Japan's own markets. On the tenth, Finance Minister Akiyo Katayama spoke about supporting the yen and about the independence of the Bank of Japan. And those comments triggered a sharp reversal. The Nikkei calls it a swing back from what markets had been calling the "Kotai Shock" — kotai simply meaning a change of guard, a handover. Ten-year Japanese government bond yields plunged by seventeen and a half basis points in the reaction. In plain terms: investors got whiplash. They're trying to read where policy is heading, and the signals keep shifting. For an export-dependent economy already worried about oil, that uncertainty is the last thing anyone wanted.

Let's turn to the front page and to Tokyo, where Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's government pushed a significant change through the Lower House.

The Nikkei reports that on the tenth, the government passed an amendment to the Imperial Household Law. Two big changes here. First, female members of the imperial family will now be allowed to keep their status even after they marry — which, under the old rules, they lost. And second, adopted sons from former branches of the imperial family will be permitted to hold succession rights.

This goes to the heart of a long, quiet crisis — the shrinking of Japan's imperial family and the very small number of male heirs. But the way it passed is drawing sharp criticism. Just three hours of debate. The opposition says the government strayed from an earlier cross-party understanding, and they're questioning whether giving succession rights to adopted male children was ever properly agreed to. So even as the substance passes, the process is under a cloud.

Staying with policy, a couple of stories that speak to how Japan is trying to shape its future — and where it's willing to bend the rules to do it.

There's real momentum behind women starting businesses. The Nikkei reports that the number of women receiving startup loans jumped more than forty-one percent year-over-year, to just under eight thousand people in fiscal 2025. Women now make up nearly thirty percent of all startup borrowers. And interestingly, it's not only the big cities leading — Yamaguchi and Tokushima prefectures both top forty percent. The paper links this to government support and to flexible self-employment that makes work-life balance more manageable, especially for mothers. It's a quiet shift away from the traditional lifetime-employment model.

But not every rule-change is being welcomed so warmly. The Diet also passed an amendment on AI and personal data — and this one's controversial. Companies will now be allowed to use personal information to train AI systems without your explicit consent up front. Instead of asking permission beforehand, the system flips to penalties after the fact if a company misuses your data. Supporters say it keeps Japan competitive in the AI race. Critics worry it quietly erodes privacy. And on that competitive theme, the government also wants to double doctoral graduates to twenty thousand a year by 2030, with research funding doubled, all aimed at AI and quantum technology.

Now to some big corporate news, because it's a fascinating one. The Nikkei reports that SoftBank and its payment arm PayPay are in talks to invest in Seven & i Holdings — that's the parent of Seven-Eleven, Japan's largest convenience store operator. We're talking about twenty-one thousand stores across the country. The idea is to bring AI into store operations and to fold that enormous footprint into the PayPay payments ecosystem. If it happens, it would knit together two of the most familiar names in daily Japanese life — the corner konbini and the phone in your pocket.

Elsewhere in business, a couple of stories that trace back to that same oil anxiety we started with. Nissan's China sales fell fifteen percent in the first half, to around 237,000 vehicles — and the paper points partly to Middle East oil price shocks crushing demand for gasoline cars, which fell a startling thirty-two percent. And Micron, the American chipmaker, is raising its long-term U.S. investment to two hundred fifty billion dollars — about two and a half trillion yen — pushed along by the Trump administration's drive for semiconductor self-sufficiency.

Looking abroad, a changing of the guard in Britain. The Nikkei reports that Andy Barnum, the former Manchester mayor, has locked up eighty percent of Labour MPs' backing and will become Prime Minister on the twentieth of July. He's promising more power to the regions and a more expansionist fiscal approach.

And two stories about the sheer weight of demographics. China has just expanded protections for its "working seniors" — guaranteeing minimum wage and workplace safety for people staying employed past sixty-five, as the country stares down a population projected to halve by the year 2100. And here's a telling detail: the United Nations has delayed its landmark world population report by a full year — because of funding shortages. At the very moment the world most needs clear data on aging and decline, the numbers themselves are running late.

Which brings me to the one that caught my eye — buried deep in the paper, in the general news pages.

It's about engine oil. Yes, engine oil. The Nikkei reports that Japan's automakers are facing an extended shortage of the high-quality engine oil that's essential to fuel efficiency. Suzuki is actually restricting how often customers can get an oil change, and it's exploring alternative formulas.

And here's what makes this more than a footnote. Japan — this titan of the automobile world — produces less than five percent of the engine oil it consumes. Everything else is imported, largely from Korea and the Middle East. So when geopolitics gets tense, or intermediaries start hoarding supply, the country that builds the cars can't reliably get the oil to run them. The government is stepping in with short-term direct supply, but the structural problem remains.

I love this story because it's such a vivid reminder of how modern economies really work. We talk endlessly about the shift to electric vehicles, about batteries and chips. And meanwhile, a humble bottle of engine oil turns out to be a genuine vulnerability at the heart of Japan's proudest industry. The big picture is made of small, overlooked things.

And on a gentler note before we go — Toyota's labor unions have secured a reworked calendar: fewer operations during the Golden Week holiday period, in exchange for more three-day weekends through the year. A small sign of a tightening labor market, and of workers asking for a little more time with their families.
