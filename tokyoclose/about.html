Tokyo Close

Architecture & How It Works

What Is This?

Tokyo Close is a fully automated daily podcast that wraps the Tokyo Stock Exchange session after the market closes. Every episode is generated end-to-end by AI — no human writes, records, or edits anything. It runs automatically at 17:17 JST every day, shortly after the Tokyo close at 15:30 JST.

Two AI hosts — Alex (British male, macro-focused) and Maya (American female, stock-picker) — discuss what actually happened in the day's session: which indices moved and by how much, which stocks led the gains and losses, and what drove it all. Where Japan was closed for a holiday or it's a weekend, the episode is a short bulletin instead of a full wrap.

Tokyo Close is a companion show to Tokyo Open, which previews the same market before the session begins.

Pipeline

Each episode is produced in five stages, taking roughly 10–15 minutes from start to finish.

1
Market Data Collection
Actual closing prices and percentage changes are pulled from Yahoo Finance after the session ends. This covers the Nikkei 225, TOPIX, and 41 individual Japanese stocks from the same universe tracked by Tokyo Open. USD/JPY, crude oil, gold, and the previous US close are also fetched for context. Takes ~15 seconds.
2
News Collection
A headless browser authenticates with a major Japanese news publication and collects articles from the past 12 hours. Six international RSS feeds are also polled in parallel. This gives the script generation step the day's market-moving news to explain the session's moves. Takes ~4 minutes.
3
News Digest (Claude Haiku)
All collected articles are passed to Claude Haiku, which produces a structured six-section briefing covering market movers, macro and FX, sector themes, key events, surprises and risks, and analyst ratings. The same pipeline as Tokyo Open, but focused on post-session explanatory context rather than pre-market catalysts. Takes ~10 seconds.
4
Script Generation (Claude Opus)
Actual closing data, the news digest, and a context block of US prices from the prior session are sent to Claude Opus, which writes a 600–800 word two-host dialogue. Alex and Maya discuss the day's session, cite specific movers with their percentage closes, and explain what drove the moves based on the day's news. Takes ~30 seconds.
5
Voice Synthesis & Publish
The script is split into individual speaker lines and each is synthesised using Google Cloud Text-to-Speech — en-GB-Journey-D for Alex and en-US-Journey-F for Maya. All segments are stitched together with silence gaps using ffmpeg and the final MP3 is pushed to this site. Takes ~8 minutes.

Broadcast Modes

The script adapts automatically based on the trading calendar.

Trading Day
Full 600–800 word wrap. Alex and Maya review actual closing prices for all major indices and key movers, explain the session's themes, and close with a look-ahead to the US open.
Holiday
Short 60–90 word bulletin. Names the holiday (e.g. Marine Day), confirms the market was closed, and tells listeners when the next trading session opens. Redirects to The Papers Say for the day's Japan news.
Weekend
Short 60–90 word bulletin. Confirms the market is closed for the weekend and tells listeners when trading resumes Monday morning.

Automation

The entire pipeline runs on GitHub Actions — no dedicated server required. A scheduled trigger fires at 17:17 JST every weekday and weekend. On trading days, the full wrap is generated; on holidays and weekends, a short bulletin is produced instead. The final MP3 is pushed directly to this GitHub Pages site, overwriting latest.mp3. The episode date metadata is updated in episode.json, which the player reads to display the correct date.

All market data is sourced from public APIs. News is pulled from publicly available RSS feeds and a licensed Japanese news publication. This project has no affiliation with any of the companies, exchanges, or publications mentioned.
