# The Mariner's Craft

### Finding your way by sky, sea, and reckoning — a practical book of traditional navigation

This is a working manual, not a history. It teaches the skills a deep-water
sailor of the age of sail actually used to cross an ocean and find a small island
at the far end of it — starting from **no prior knowledge** and building, chapter
by chapter, to a complete day's work in navigation. Every technique here was used
at sea for real; several kept sailors alive for centuries before electronics.

It is **self-contained**: Part 0 begins from zero (what latitude and longitude
even are, and how to read the sky-dome), so you need nothing before it.

## How to read it

Read the parts in order — each builds on the last, starting from **Part 0**. The four computational
chapters (the noon sight, the time sight, the intercept method, and the sailings)
carry **worked examples with real numbers**; do them with pencil and paper the
first time and they'll stick. Everything else is procedure and judgement you can
read straight through.

Where a chapter needs numbers from a **nautical almanac** (the Sun's and stars'
positions for a given instant) the text says so plainly and shows the shape of
the calculation. You don't need an almanac to *understand* the method; you need
one to *shoot* a real sight.

## What you'd want in your hands

- A **sextant** (or, to learn, your own hand at arm's length — Part I)
- An accurate **clock set to Greenwich time** (a chronometer, or today a phone)
- A **nautical almanac** and **sight-reduction tables** (or a calculator)
- A **magnetic compass** and the ship's **deviation card**
- A **chip log** or knotted line, and a watch, to measure speed
- A **lead line** for soundings
- **Mercator charts** of where you're going, and a parallel rule and dividers
- A clear horizon and a patient eye

---

*A note on honesty:* real navigation is unforgiving of sloppy arithmetic and
optimistic assumptions. The methods here are correct and usable, but at sea you
check everything twice, distrust a single sight, and treat your dead reckoning as
the truth until the sky proves otherwise. That habit of doubt is itself part of
the craft.
